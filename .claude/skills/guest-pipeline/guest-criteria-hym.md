# HYM (Hack You) Guest Qualification Criteria

## Show Profile

Hack You is a health, wellness, and human performance podcast that brings on credentialed experts who can translate complex science into actionable advice. The show sits at the intersection of evidence-based medicine and internet-native health culture, attracting guests who have both real credentials and the ability to engage a digitally savvy audience.

**Audience:** Health-conscious adults (25-45) who actively seek to optimize their bodies and minds. They follow health creators on YouTube and Instagram, buy supplements, track biomarkers, and want to go deeper than surface-level wellness content. Skews male but not exclusively.

**Host context:** The show records both remotely and in-person (Dubai, London, LA). Guests should be comfortable in long-form conversation and able to go deep on their domain while keeping it accessible.

## The Hack You Guest Profile

The ideal HYM guest is a **credentialed expert with a content engine**. They hold real qualifications (MD, PhD, published research, clinical practice) but they also create content, have social followings, and speak the language of internet health culture. They bridge the gap between peer-reviewed science and the conversations people are actually having about their health.

**Think:** The doctor or scientist your audience already follows on Instagram or YouTube, the one whose name comes up in every biohacking subreddit, the researcher who goes on podcasts because they genuinely want to educate, not because their PR team arranged it.

**NOT:** Boring clinical practitioners who have credentials but zero online presence, generic dietitians with conventional advice, wellness influencers with no scientific grounding, or pure academics who cannot communicate outside of journal papers.

## What Makes a Good Guest

### Must-haves
- Genuine credentials in their domain (MD, PhD, published research, clinical practice, or equivalent depth of expertise)
- Active online presence with content that demonstrates their ability to communicate complex topics clearly
- A specific area of expertise or a unique angle that differentiates them from generic health voices
- Comfortable in long-form podcast conversation, able to go deep without losing the audience
- Brand-safe while still being willing to challenge mainstream health narratives with evidence

### Minimum Thresholds
- 50K+ followers across their strongest platform (Instagram, YouTube, X, or TikTok). This is a guideline rather than a hard cutoff: a researcher with 30K but exceptional depth and a growing trajectory can work, while someone with 200K who posts nothing but generic wellness tips does not.
- Must have a verifiable professional profile (LinkedIn, university page, clinic, or published work) in addition to social presence
- Prioritize US, UK, Dubai, and Australia-based guests given recording logistics, with remote-friendly international guests also welcome

### The "Trendy" Test
Before adding any candidate, ask: "Is this person part of the current health conversation, or are they a conventional practitioner who happens to be qualified?" Apply these filters:
- **Trendy means:** They have a point of view that generates discussion. They create content people share. They appear on other health/performance podcasts. Their work is cited in online health communities. They represent a movement or methodology (carnivore, longevity, biohacking, gut health revolution, hormone optimization) rather than just dispensing standard medical advice.
- **Not trendy means:** Their credentials are solid but their communication style is clinical and dry. They have no content strategy. Their social media is dormant or purely promotional. They would blend into any hospital's staff directory without standing out.

### The Accessibility Test
- **Too big:** Peter Attia, Andrew Huberman, Matthew Walker at peak demand, Bryan Johnson (these are aspirational targets, not realistic pipeline candidates unless a direct connection exists). Keep them as stretch goals, not standard pipeline entries.
- **Sweet spot:** 50K-2M followers, actively doing podcast appearances, building their media presence, and genuinely benefiting from exposure to Hack You's audience. Think: the guest who says yes because they want to reach more people, not because their agent negotiated it.
- **Too small/too niche:** Under 50K with no trajectory, academics who have never done a podcast, practitioners whose expertise is so narrow it cannot fill a compelling episode.

## Guest Lanes

### Core Lanes
1. **Nutrition Science and Metabolism** - Evidence-based nutrition researchers, sports nutritionists with academic backing, metabolic health experts who translate research into practical eating strategies. This is the show's strongest lane and should always be well-represented. Examples from the database: Alan Aragon, Layne Norton, Brad Schoenfeld, Eric Helms, Christopher Barakat, Stuart Phillips, Bill Campbell, Adrian Chavez. Examples of targets: Chris Masterjohn, Shawn Stevenson, Thomas DeLauer, Ben Azadi, Paul Saladino.

2. **Longevity and Anti-Aging** - Researchers and practitioners working on extending healthspan and lifespan, from cellular biology to practical interventions. The biohacking and longevity space is a major draw for the audience. Examples from database: Valter Longo, David Sinclair, Aubrey de Grey, Morgan Levine, Brian Kennedy, Dale Bredesen. Examples of targets: Bryan Johnson, Dan Buettner, Eric Verdin, Darshan Shah.

3. **Functional and Integrative Medicine** - Doctors who practice outside conventional medicine with evidence-based protocols, particularly those with strong content presence and a methodology their audience can follow. Examples from database: Dr. Mark Hyman, Dr. Will Cole, Dr. Josh Axe, Dr. Steven Gundry, Dr. Jason Fung, Dr. Mindy Pelz, Dr. Sara Gottfried, Dr. Jolene Brighten. Examples of targets: Dr. Ben Lynch, Dr. Kara Fitzgerald, Dr. Zach Bush.

4. **Exercise Science and Performance** - PhDs and practitioners in exercise physiology, hypertrophy, strength science, and athletic performance who create content. Not just fitness influencers, but people with genuine research credentials who also know how to communicate. Examples from database: Dr. Andy Galpin, Dr. Milo Wolf, Brad Schoenfeld, Menno Henselmans, Pat Davidson, Kelly Starrett, Dr. Aaron Horschig. Examples of targets: Jeremy Ethier, Jeff Nippard, Stan Efferding.

5. **Biohacking and Optimization** - Tech-forward health optimizers, wearable technology founders, and practitioners who push the boundary of what is considered mainstream health. They should have real data or methodology behind their claims, not just hype. Examples from database: Dave Asprey, Tim Gray, Ryan Smith, Dr. Adeel Khan, Dr. Molly Maloof. Examples of targets: Brigham Buhler, Ben Greenfield, Siim Land.

### Secondary Lanes
6. **Gut Health and Microbiome** - Researchers and practitioners specializing in the gut-health connection with strong communication skills. A rapidly growing area of health interest that the audience actively engages with. Examples from database: Will Bulsiewicz, Megan Rossi, Michael Ruscio, Dr. Rupy Aujla, Simon J Hill. Examples of targets: Emeran Mayer, Jonathan Wolf.

7. **Brain Science, Sleep, and Mental Health** - Neuroscientists, sleep researchers, psychiatrists, and psychologists who translate brain science into protocols people can use. Must be accessible communicators, not pure academics. Examples from database: Dr. Rabin (Apollo Neuroscience), Anna Lembke, Caroline Leaf, Dr. Tara Swart, Matthew Walker. Examples of targets: Dr. Daniel Amen, Lisa Mosconi, Michael Breus, Dr. Paul Conti.

8. **Hormone Health and Women's/Men's Health** - Endocrinologists, hormone specialists, and practitioners focused on gender-specific health optimization. This includes testosterone optimization, menopause, fertility, and hormonal balance. Examples from database: Dr. Kyle Gillett, Dr. Gabrielle Lyon, Dr. Stephanie Estima, Louise Newson, Dr. Felice Gersh, Dr. Amy Shah. Examples of targets: Benjamin Bikman (insulin focus), Dr. Jeremy London.

### Bonus Lanes
9. **Physique and Strength Crossover** - Elite strength athletes or physique competitors who also have depth in health, science, or business beyond their sport. Not just "big guy lifts heavy things" but someone who can hold a conversation about the science of what they do. Examples from database: Chris Bumstead, Eddie Hall, Brian Shaw, Robert Oberst, Ben Pakulski, Mark Bell, Vigorous Steve, Jordan Syatt.

10. **Medical Mavericks and Controversial Health Voices** - Practitioners or researchers who challenge mainstream medical consensus with evidence and data, not just contrarianism. They spark debate and drive engagement because they represent alternative approaches that a significant audience believes in. Examples from database: Dr. Jack Kruse, Anthony Chaffee, Ken D Berry, Barbara O'Neill, Joel Fuhrman. Handle with care: they should be evidence-informed, not pseudoscience promoters.

## Hard Exclusions
- **Generic dietitians and nutritionists** without a unique angle, content presence, or research credentials that set them apart from thousands of others in their field
- **Conventional clinical practitioners** who are competent but have no online presence, content strategy, or ability to draw an audience
- **Pure wellness influencers** with large followings but no credentials, no methodology, and no substance behind the content (smoothie bowl creators, yoga-pose Instagrammers, crystal healers)
- **Academics who cannot communicate** outside of journal papers, regardless of how impressive their research is
- **Health MLM promoters** or anyone whose primary business model is selling supplements through network marketing
- **Celebrities or athletes** whose only connection to health is that they "got fit" for a role or have a supplement line they do not understand
- **Anyone with a history of dangerous medical misinformation** (anti-vax activism, promoting unproven cures for serious diseases, or making claims that could directly harm people following their advice)
- **Duplicate guests** already in the Airtable (185+ records, always deduplicate before adding)

## Calibration: Understanding the Show's DNA

Study the guest database patterns carefully. The show has a clear identity:
- **Credentials matter more than follower count.** Unlike entertainment podcasts, Hack You guests need real qualifications. An MD with 80K followers who published groundbreaking metabolic research is more valuable than a wellness influencer with 500K who repackages other people's content.
- **The "internet doctor" archetype dominates.** The vast majority of successful guests are MDs, PhDs, or published researchers who built content brands. Dr. Andy Galpin, Layne Norton, Brad Schoenfeld, Dr. Milo Wolf, Anthony Chaffee, Will Bulsiewicz are all credentialed experts with active content channels.
- **Nutrition and metabolism are the centre of gravity.** More guests come from this lane than any other, reflecting the audience's core interest.
- **Longevity and biohacking are the growth edge.** The spotlight/priority list skews toward longevity (Dan Buettner, Benjamin Bikman) and biohacking, suggesting the show is leaning into this territory.
- **Dubai/international recording** is a real factor. 15 guests are tagged Dubai, and the show records in multiple locations, so geographic flexibility matters.
- **The show avoids pure entertainment.** No comedians, no lifestyle vloggers, no motivational speakers. Every guest brings domain expertise.

### Status Distribution (for pipeline calibration)
- 21 completed recordings confirm the quality bar
- 6 spotlight/priority guests represent the aspirational tier
- 48 reached-out guests show the conversion funnel
- The pipeline runs deep (185 total), so deduplication against existing records is critical

## Diversity Requirement
Each run MUST produce a mix across categories. Do not load up on a single lane (e.g., all nutrition scientists). The show covers nutrition, longevity, exercise science, biohacking, gut health, brain science, and hormone health. Aim for representation across at least 5 of the lane categories per run. Nutrition science can have the highest representation (it is the show's strongest lane) but should not exceed 25% of any single run.
