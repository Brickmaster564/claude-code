## Newsletter 1: Guest Story Arc + Episode Teaser

**Input type:** Podcast episode with guest (Alex Cooper, Electric Twin)
**Angle:** Unconventional career path, surprising turns, one standout quote

```
This founder's path makes no sense on paper...

History degree at Oxford, trader in the City, and then one day he walked past an army recruiting centre, walked in, and resigned from his job the same week.

Alex Cooper thought he'd do two years...

Then 9/11 happened. Twenty years in the military followed.

Intense Arabic training, operational tours, the lot.

When he finally left, COVID hit, and he ended up running the operational side of the UK's testing programme. That's where he met his co-founder, Ben Warner, who was the PM's chief advisor on data and digital.

Now they're building Electric Twin, using AI to simulate how customers actually think and behave.

In this episode of the pod, we talked about:

Why formal education is becoming irrelevant (and what to hire for instead)
Why "balance" is bullshit.
Fundraising without the drama.
The hiring filter that saved them.

One line that stuck with me:

"If you're optimising for wellness, you're probably not doing anything worth remembering."

Agree or disagree, it's worth a listen.

Watch the full episode here.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with intrigue, not the guest's name or company. The path "makes no sense" is the hook.
- Walks through the career arc in rapid-fire, short paragraphs. Each turn is surprising.
- Bullet list of topics is scannable, gives the reader four reasons to click.
- The standout quote is set apart and framed as opinion-worthy ("agree or disagree"), which invites engagement.
- CTA is one line, no pressure.

## Newsletter 2: Guest Credentials + Topic Menu

**Input type:** Podcast episode with guest (Nick Holzherr, Whisk/GitLaw)
**Angle:** Impressive track record, multiple compelling topics, strong thesis

```
Nick Holzherr has really done it all.

He's sold companies, bought companies, invested in over 20 startups...

AND he's taken Whisk from zero to 120 people - all before selling it to Samsung in 2019.

He also runs fully distributed teams. Always has.

Most people think that's mental, but for Nick it's the only way to scale fast (if you're not Google or Facebook, and let's face it, you aren't).

This conversation is about why fully remote beats office-based (with data arguments on both sides), why async work is the actual operating system for high-performing teams, and why the majority of people are drastically undervaluing AI.

Nick's currently building GitLaw - an AI agent that drafts and reviews legal documents.

He's seeing what 15 people can do when they're doing the work of 50 - all without the communication overhead that slows down a team of that size.

This is some of what we go over:

· Why hiring globally for top 5% talent beats hiring locally for "pretty good" talent

· The three-to-four-hour time zone rule (Korea to San Francisco was too extreme)

· How async work creates perfect context for AI

· Why his original leadership team is still with him 15 years later

· Why legal services being accessible only to rich companies is fundamentally unfair

There's also a brilliant moment about measuring productivity in distributed teams (you can't rely on "they bring donuts and make coffee" anymore), and why the bottleneck for AI isn't technology - it's having really smart people who can judge when the AI is right and when it's wildly wrong.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens by establishing credibility fast with specific achievements (not vague "serial entrepreneur")
- "AND" in caps for emphasis on the biggest achievement, natural and conversational
- The parenthetical jab ("let's face it, you aren't") adds personality and breaks formality
- Topic list uses middle dots (·) and each item is a mini-hook on its own
- Closes with a teaser moment, not a hard CTA. Makes you curious enough to click.
- No "click here to listen" push. The content sells itself.

## Newsletter 3: Personal Insight + Video CTA

**Input type:** Solo YouTube video (Founder's Curve / No Man's Land)
**Angle:** Pattern recognition, emotional resonance, named concept

```
There's a pattern that every founder hits on their way up.

The thing that catches them all? A stage I call No Man's Land.

Sounds scary, right? Well, I've scaled two firms to £30 million, coached over 100 founder-CEOs, and the story's always the same:

You're around 70 or 80 people. Suddenly profit's dropping, growth is stalling, and you're burning out.

One founder described it to me like this:

"I wake up, work all day, come home exhausted, and feel like I've achieved nothing. The business isn't moving. I'm questioning everything I'm doing."

First session together, he broke down and asked me: "Fix this in six months or help me sell because I can't keep doing this."

That's No Man's Land.

You've been playing one game, and suddenly the rules have changed. But you're still playing the old way.

I've just released a new video on this: The Seven Stages of the Founder's Curve.

It maps out the journey from starting up to the final decision point: break through or decline.

Most founders hit this wall without seeing it coming, but when you understand the pattern, you can prepare for it and navigate through.

If you're scaling past 50 people and something's starting to feel a bit harder than it should, watch this:
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with a universal pattern, not "I made a new video"
- "No Man's Land" is a named concept that people will remember and use
- Credentials are woven in naturally ("I've scaled two firms to £30M, coached over 100 founder-CEOs") rather than listed
- The direct quote from a founder is raw and emotional, the reader feels it
- Solo video content is framed as a solution to a problem the reader might have right now
- CTA is conditional: "If you're scaling past 50 people..." which qualifies the reader and makes it feel personal

## Newsletter 4: Guest Contrarian Insight + Product Teaser

**Input type:** Podcast episode with guest (Nik Setting, personal branding / Mochi)
**Angle:** Contrarian take on the creator economy, strong quote, product as proof point

```
Most creators are stuck in a race to show more stuff.

Bigger cars, better edits, flashier lifestyles... and every time they level up, it stops being enough. So they go again.

Nik Setting thinks this only attracts one type of person.

Broke people.

He helps creators and founders build personal brands that actually convert. His method is what he calls the Profile Funnel. Optimize your profile, your content, and your DM conversations so the brand itself does the selling.

He also just launched Mochi, an AI tool that lets creators run thousands of personalized DM conversations at scale (Forbes featured it).

And half the deals he signs? Through the DMs. No sales calls, just conversations.

We got into a lot in this one:

· "Just post consistently" as branding advice... and why it's wrong
· The difference between documented and undocumented content (your audience can feel which one you're making)
· Niching down as the fastest path to brand authority, even when it feels limiting
· What "conversion communication" means and why it might be the most valuable skill right now
· Going hardest when things are working, not when they're struggling
· The hiring filter that matters more than skill or experience

One line that stuck with me:

"If you show a system or the golden script, you will attract broke people. If you show a Lambo, you will attract broke people. A billionaire doesn't care about a Lambo, he has 17 of them."

Watch the full episode here.

P.S. The bit about documented vs undocumented content is worth the watch alone.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with a universal observation ("race to show more stuff") that any creator or business owner feels, not with the guest's name
- "Broke people." sits alone as a punchline. White space around it lets it land.
- Ellipsis on "flashier lifestyles..." creates a trailing pause before the turn
- "And half the deals he signs?" uses a question as a natural conversational break mid-paragraph
- Parentheticals add personality without breaking flow: "(Forbes featured it)", "(your audience can feel which one you're making)"
- Bullets vary their opening words and each one is a standalone reason to click
- The P.S. picks one specific moment and sells it as worth the watch on its own
- Conversational connectors throughout: "And half the deals...", "We got into a lot in this one"

## Structural DNA: How Nalu Newsletters Are Built

### Two input types, same approach

**Podcast episode (with guest):**
- Lead with the guest's most interesting trait, achievement, or career turn
- Walk through enough backstory to make the reader care
- List 3-6 topics covered as scannable bullets (each one a mini-hook)
- Pull one standout quote or moment and set it apart
- CTA to watch/listen

**Solo video (host's own content):**
- Lead with the insight or problem, not the video announcement
- Share enough of the content to hook, not enough to satisfy
- Use a real story or quote to make the concept tangible
- CTA framed as "if this applies to you, watch this"

### Newsletter structure

1. **Hook** - The most interesting or surprising thing. Not "New episode out" or "This week on the pod." The hook earns the read.

2. **Context / backstory** - Just enough to give the hook weight. For guests: career arc, credentials, what they're building. For solo content: the pattern, the problem, the setup.

3. **Topic bullets** - Scannable list of what's covered. Each bullet should be interesting enough to click on its own. Use middle dots (·) or dashes (-).

4. **Standout moment** - One quote, one insight, one line that makes the reader stop. Set it apart with line breaks and quotation marks.

5. **CTA** - Simple, one line. "Watch the full episode here." or "If you're [qualifier], watch this:" No multi-paragraph sell.

### Pacing rules

- Short paragraphs throughout. 1-2 sentences per block.
- Line breaks between every thought.
- The newsletter should feel like it takes 60-90 seconds to read. Enough to hook, not enough to replace the episode.
- Ellipsis (...) used sparingly to create pause or trailing thought. Not overused.

### Conversational texture

These are the tools that make a newsletter feel human, not structured:

- **Ellipsis for trailing thought.** "He's sold companies, bought companies, invested in over 20 startups..." The pause implies there's even more.
- **Punchline on its own line.** A short, sharp line that lands harder because of the white space around it. "Broke people." or "That's No Man's Land."
- **Questions as conversational breaks.** "And half the deals he signs?" or "Sounds scary, right?" Breaks the rhythm and pulls the reader forward.
- **Parenthetical asides.** Add personality without derailing flow. "(Forbes featured it)", "(let's face it, you aren't)", "(your audience can feel which one you're making)."
- **"And" sentence starters.** "And half the deals he signs?" or "AND he's taken Whisk from zero to 120 people." Casual, conversational, mimics how people actually tell stories.
- **Bullet variation.** Each bullet in a topic list should start with a different word. Varying the structure keeps the eye moving.
- **P.S. as a second hook.** Don't waste the P.S. on a generic closer. Pick one specific moment from the episode and sell it as worth the click on its own.

### What newsletters do NOT do

- Don't open with "This week on the podcast..." or any variant of announcing content first
- Don't summarise the entire episode. Tease, don't satisfy.
- Don't list every topic covered. Pick the 3-6 most compelling.
- Don't use formal transitions between sections. Just break to a new line.
- Don't end with "Hope you enjoy it" or similar weak closers.
