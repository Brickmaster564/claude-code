# Nalu Copywriting Bible

Universal voice, style, and structural rules for all Nalu client copy. This is the reference for how Nalu copy sounds, reads, and flows, regardless of the client, niche, or platform.

---

## The Voice

Nalu copy sounds like a smart person talking to you over coffee. Not performing. Not selling. Just sharing something they genuinely think matters.

### Core traits

- **Conversational, not literary.** Write how people talk. First person. Contractions. Fragments where they sound natural.
- **Vulnerable, not polished.** The writer admits mistakes, shares doubts, calls themselves out. This is what makes the reader trust the bigger point.
- **Direct, not motivational.** No "you've got this" fluff. State the thing plainly. If the insight is strong, it doesn't need cheerleading.
- **Specific, not vague.** Real numbers, real names, real situations. "235 members paying £49 per month" beats "hundreds of paying customers."
- **Universal, not niche.** Even when the topic is complex (M&A, AI, scaling), the caption makes it accessible to anyone scrolling past. High ceiling, wide floor.
- **UK English, global audience.** Casual UK tone (realised, travelling, arse) but no slang that would confuse an international reader.

### What the voice is NOT

- Not corporate. No "leveraging synergies" or "driving impact."
- Not motivational speaker. No "rise and grind" or "you've got this, king."
- Not academic. No citations, frameworks named for show, or hedging every claim.
- Not salesy. Even when there's a CTA, it feels like sharing something useful, not pushing a product.

---

## The Golden Rule: High Ceiling, Wide Floor

Every caption must work at two levels:

1. **Wide floor:** Someone with zero context on the topic can read it, understand the core lesson, and feel something.
2. **High ceiling:** Someone deep in the niche recognises the insight as genuinely sharp and worth sharing.

This means: take the complex version, strip the jargon, find the universal human truth underneath, and lead with that.

**Example:** A podcast about M&A deal structures becomes a caption about "the moment you realise your business owns you, not the other way around." The M&A detail is the vehicle. The human truth is the destination.

## Structural DNA

### How captions are built

Nalu captions follow a loose but consistent structure:

1. **Hook** - A bold claim, a question, a raw statement, or a surprising fact. Must work standalone. No throat-clearing, no "I've been thinking about..." openers.

2. **Story or observation** - The personal experience, case study, or moment that gives the hook weight. This is where specifics live: names, numbers, places, dates, emotions.

3. **The turn** - The pivot where the insight lands. Often starts with "But", "Then I realised", "Here's the thing", or similar. This is where the reader goes from "interesting story" to "oh, that changes how I think."

4. **The takeaway** - The universal lesson, reframe, or call to reflection. Should feel earned by the story, not bolted on. One sentence is often enough.

5. **CTA (optional)** - If there's something to watch, read, or do, state it simply. No hard sell.

### Structural rules

- **One idea per caption.** If the transcript has five great moments, write five captions. Don't cram.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-2 sentences per block. Line breaks between every thought.
- **Length adapts to content.** A powerful reframe might be 50 words. A stage-by-stage walkthrough might be 300. The content dictates the length, not a formula.
- **Scannable formatting.** Use arrows (→), bullets (-), or numbered lists when presenting steps, stages, or multiple points. Makes it easy to skim on mobile.

## Sentence Rules

### Do this

- Mix short and medium sentences. Natural rhythm, like speech.
- Let some sentences be fragments. "And I never will." works on its own.
- Use questions to pull the reader in. "Does that mean you're a leader?"
- Let the punchline sit on its own line with white space around it.

### Conversational texture (applies to all formats)

These are the tools that make copy feel human:

- **Ellipsis for trailing thought.** "He's sold companies, bought companies, invested in over 20 startups..." The pause implies more. Use sparingly, not every paragraph.
- **Punchline isolation.** A short line that lands because of the white space around it. "Broke people." or "That's No Man's Land." or "And I never will."
- **Questions as rhythm breaks.** "And half the deals he signs?" or "Sounds scary, right?" Breaks the cadence and pulls the reader forward naturally.
- **Parenthetical asides.** Inject personality without derailing flow. "(Forbes featured it)", "(let's face it, you aren't)", "(your audience can feel which one you're making)."
- **"And" as a sentence starter.** Casual, conversational, mimics how people tell stories. "And half the deals he signs?" or "And every time they level up, it stops being enough."
- **Commas as list rhythm.** "Bigger cars, better edits, flashier lifestyles" reads smoother than three separate fragments.

### Do NOT do this

- **No staccato sentences.** Never use 2-3 word sentences in sequence. "Bold move. Game changer. Think bigger." is banned. It reads like a motivational poster, not a person talking.
- **No run-on sentences.** If a sentence needs three commas and two semicolons, break it up.
- **No repeated sentence starts.** If three lines in a row begin with "I" or "The" or "It", rewrite at least one.

## Hook Patterns

Hooks that work across Nalu clients, extracted from proven captions:

| Pattern | Example | Why it works |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Bold contrarian claim** | "I've NEVER asked my son what he wants to be when he grows up." | Violates expectation, creates curiosity gap |
| **Direct question** | "If every issue between your best people lands on your desk, does that mean you're a leader?" | Implicates the reader, they have to answer |
| **Raw reaction** | "War today, what a pain." | Unfiltered honesty signals authenticity |
| **"This is crazy" opener** | "This is crazy. I met an entrepreneur who was a UX designer." | Energy + specificity, promises a story |
| **Important reminder** | "Important reminder. You're not falling behind on AI." | Names the reader's anxiety, then relieves it |
| **Universal truth, then deflation** | "Every founder thinks this about their own scale-up journey: It will be different. But guess what? It won't be." | Reader sees themselves, then gets humbled |

### The contextualise-first rule

Hooks must call out the audience before delivering the lesson. The viewer needs to self-select in the first few words, then the insight lands on someone who already knows it's for them.

**Good:** "If you're a business owner and your team is underperforming, they're probably missing at least three of these."
**Bad:** "Most leadership teams have zero real trust and here are the five behaviors that prove it."

The good version contextualises ("if you're a business owner"), names their situation ("team is underperforming"), then opens the loop ("at least three of these"). The bad version makes a statement about other people's teams. The viewer doesn't feel implicated.

Pattern: **[Audience call-out] + [their situation/pain] + [the hook/loop]**

This applies to captions, scripts, and newsletters. The wider the audience call-out, the higher the ceiling. "If you lead a team" is wider than "If you're a founder-CEO scaling past £3M." Use the widest version that's still honest.

### Hook blacklist

- "I've been thinking about..."
- "Here's the thing about [topic]..."
- "In today's world..."
- "Let's talk about..."
- Any opener that delays the point
- Hooks that make statements about other people without implicating the viewer

## The Turn

The turn is where the caption becomes worth reading. It's the moment the story pivots into insight.

### Turn patterns

- **Self-correction:** "Then I realised what a selfish ass-hole I was being."
- **Reframe:** "You're not leading, you're mediating."
- **Contrast:** "If a kid says 'I want to solve loneliness'... that opens up hundreds of paths. If that same kid says 'I want to be a lawyer'... they've just closed every door except one."
- **Named concept:** "Next comes No Man's Land." (Giving a stage or problem a name makes it sticky.)
- **The quiet punchline:** "It's because the founder can't make the shift from hero to architect."

The turn should feel inevitable in hindsight but surprising in the moment.

---

## CTA Rules

- Not every caption needs a CTA. If the insight stands alone, let it.
- When there is a CTA, keep it simple: "I just released a new video that shows you how."
- Never hard sell. The caption earned attention through value. The CTA is an invitation, not a pitch.
- "Link in bio", "Drop a comment", "Save this" are fine when used sparingly.

---

## Formatting for Platforms

### LinkedIn
- Line breaks between every paragraph
- Arrows (→) for step-by-step lists
- No hashtags in the body, if used at all, place at the bottom
- No emojis (or at most one, used meaningfully)

### Instagram
- Same line break rules
- Hashtags in first comment or at the very end
- Can be slightly more casual/personal than LinkedIn

### X (Twitter)
- Shorter overall, respect the character limit
- Thread format for longer pieces
- Same voice rules apply, just compressed

## Content-to-Caption Translation

When working from a transcript:

1. **Find the single best moment.** The one insight, story, or reframe that would make someone stop scrolling.
2. **Ignore everything else.** One caption, one idea. If there are three great moments, write three captions.
3. **Translate, don't transcribe.** The transcript is the raw material. The caption is a new piece of writing that captures the essence in a format built for scrolling.
4. **Make it universal.** The transcript might be about a specific industry. The caption finds the human truth underneath and leads with that.
5. **Keep the speaker's voice.** If the speaker swears, let them swear. If they're dry and understated, match that. The caption should sound like them at their most articulate, not like a copywriter rewrote them.

---

## Content-to-Newsletter Translation

When working from a podcast or solo video transcript:

1. **Find the hook.** The guest's most surprising trait, the boldest claim, the pattern that hits home. Lead with that, not "new episode out."
2. **Give just enough backstory.** For guests: career arc, credentials, what they're building. For solo content: the problem, the setup, enough to make the reader care.
3. **List 3-6 topics as scannable bullets.** Each one a mini-hook. Use middle dots (·) or dashes.
4. **Pull one standout moment.** A quote, a line, a reframe. Set it apart with white space.
5. **CTA in one line.** "Watch the full episode here." For solo content, qualify the reader: "If you're scaling past 50 people, watch this:"
6. **Don't summarise the whole episode.** Tease, don't satisfy. The newsletter should make them want more, not feel like they've already got it.

### Podcast vs. solo video nuances

Both follow the same structure. The difference:
- **Podcast:** The guest is the character. Their story, credentials, and quotes carry the newsletter.
- **Solo video:** The host's insight is the character. A named concept, a framework, a pattern they've observed. Often uses a client story or real example to make it tangible.

## Content-to-Script Translation

When working from a transcript, produce **5-6 short-form scripts** as a batch:

1. **Script 1 is always the compressed version.** The entire video condensed into one fast, actionable short. Rapidfire numbered list. Every key point gets one line.
2. **Scripts 2-6 are standalone lessons.** Each one pulls a different insight, story, or takeaway. Each has standalone value for someone who never watches the full video.

### Rules for every script

- **2-3 hook options per script.** Different angles for the same content. Test which wins.
- **Open loops throughout.** The hook creates the first loop. Then scatter more mid-script: trailing thoughts, rhetorical pauses, "let me explain", challenged assumptions. The viewer should never feel like they've "got it" until the payoff.
- **One lesson per script.** If you're tempted to cram two insights into one script, make two scripts.
- **Written for spoken delivery.** Read it out loud. If it stumbles, rewrite. One thought per line.
- **CTA at the end.** Every script points somewhere: follow, link, watch the full video.

### Script type mix

| Type | What it does | Pacing |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Rapidfire / Compressed | Entire video condensed, numbered list | Fast |
| Single lesson deep-dive | One insight via metaphor, analogy, or story | Medium |
| Standalone takeaway | One specific actionable lesson | Short, punchy |
| Promo / CTA-heavy | Lead with pain, end with offer (if applicable) | Pain slow, offer fast |

### Open loop placement

- **Hook:** Tease bad news, promise explanation, make a claim that needs resolving
- **Mid-script:** "And the only way out?", trailing "but your role...", "And you feel important that way, but..."
- **Pre-CTA:** "Want to learn what those systems are?"

Never let the viewer feel satisfied before the end.

---

## Blacklist

Never use in any Nalu copy (captions, newsletters, scripts, announcements):

- Em dashes (use commas, full stops, or hyphens instead)
- Staccato sentences (2-3 word punchy fragments in sequence)
- "Certainly", "indeed", "delve", "navigate", "landscape"
- "Leveraging", "synergies", "driving impact", "thought leader"
- "Moreover", "furthermore", "in conclusion"
- Cringe military/slang metaphors: "war stories", "in the trenches", "battle-tested", "boots on the ground", "in the weeds", "deep dive"
- Overused slang: "hit differently", "hits different", "slaps", "fire", "game-changer", "level up"
- Rhetorical label questions: "The reality?" "The truth?" "The problem?"
- "It's not X, it's Y" or "It's not about X, it's about Y" sentence structures
- Hashtag stuffing
- Emoji chains
- More than one exclamation mark per piece
- Generic motivational closers ("You've got this", "Keep pushing", "Your time is now")
- Opening with content announcements ("This week on the pod...", "New episode out...", "Just dropped...")
