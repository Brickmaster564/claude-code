## Caption 1: Personal Reaction to World Events

**Angle:** Vulnerability, self-reflection, pivot to mission
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn/Instagram, thought leadership

```
War today, what a pain.

This morning I woke up like many of us to the news of Iran being attacked.

While trying to work out what's happening I get an email that all my TV news appearances today are cancelled.

I had a full day planned to share my positive news of hope with the world.

I thought, that's so annoying.

I had planned to share my news on the kids book coming out and how many dreamers we have helped and their success stories.

All positive news for the world, instead it's replaced by WAR and death news.

I was bummed out.

Then I realised what a selfish ass-hole I was being.

Think of the poor families and human beings being bloody bombed today!

That's the thing I should be concerned about.

Why are humans so selfish by default sometimes.

Sometimes in this crazy news cycle world we forget perspective and - FFS! humans are being killed today because of ideology differences between countries. Mostly (in my view) a communication disconnect. I know it's more complicated than that don't come after me LI worriers x

I wonder when I see this happened, what can one person (me), can do to help?

I cannot help but default to the desperate need to fix the education system.

Having travelled the world from the age of 23 and seen how similar so called different cultures are to us, I have educated myself that people are all the same.

It's only management systems that differ. (Governments).

Irans management style is the issue.

USA management system does not reflect all US citizens just as Irans doesn't.

I try not to get political as it divides us not pulls us together, so I won't give involved in the debate on if war is justified or not but I will say it makes me even more determined to fix the education system so we can unite as humans.

I will go on the news another day and try and spread my positive message. I am not getting bombed and should be grateful but hard to be grateful when you see others in pain.

We are all connected if we like it or not.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with raw, unfiltered reaction (not a polished thought)
- Admits selfishness before pivoting to empathy
- Ties a global event back to a personal mission
- No corporate framing at all, reads like a journal entry shared publicly
- The vulnerability makes the bigger point land harder

## Caption 2: Contrarian Parenting / Education

**Angle:** Bold contrarian claim, personal story, reframe
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn/Instagram, universal appeal

```
I've NEVER asked my son what he wants to be when he grows up.

And I never will.

When my dad was alive, the plan was for me to be a lawyer.

I was a kid, so I never questioned it. I thought it was what I was meant to do.

But when he died. I realised I'd never actually wanted that.

That question - "what do you want to be when you grow up?" - puts a kid in a box before they've even started living.

Lawyer. Doctor. Accountant. A 7-year-old isn't ready to pick.

The question I ask my son is different: "What problem do you want to solve?"

Because if a kid says "I want to solve loneliness"... that opens up hundreds of paths.

Technology. Psychology. Community. Content. Charity. Hospitality.

If that same kid says "I want to be a lawyer"... they've just closed every door except one.

Schools don't ask the right question. Because schools weren't built to create problem solvers. They were built to create workers.

That's why I pulled my son out. And it's why I wrote this book.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with a bold, slightly provocative statement that creates curiosity
- Personal backstory gives the claim weight
- Reframes a universal experience (being asked what you want to be) with a better question
- The contrast (solve loneliness = hundreds of paths vs. lawyer = one door) is the punchline
- Ties back to action the writer took (pulled son out, wrote a book) without being salesy

## Caption 3: Leadership Reframe + Structured Solution

**Angle:** Challenge assumption, provide framework, CTA to content
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn, B2B leadership

```
Let me ask you something.

If every issue between your best people lands on your desk, does that mean that you're a leader?

The answer's no. You're not leading, you're mediating.

And here's the issue:

It means your A players won't challenge each other on just about anything.

They know better and just come to you.

I've just released a new video that shows you how to change that in 90 days, and the plan is simple:

→ Month 1: Build trust so your leaders can tell the truth without coming to you first.

→ Month 2: Turn that trust into conflict so hard conversations happen peer-to-peer, not through you.

→ Month 3: Install the rhythm so decisions get made once and actions get owned.

The tool that makes the biggest change? No Triangulation.

This means no conversations about anybody else on the team if they're not in the room.

Instead, you give them 72 hours to have the conversation themselves or you tell the other person what was said.

It kills gossip in a matter of weeks.

Building a team that runs without you is all about building an environment where great people can do their jobs without you becoming the bottleneck.

So stop being the referee.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens with a direct question that implicates the reader
- Reframes a behaviour they think is good (handling issues) as actually bad (mediating)
- The 90-day plan uses arrows for scannable structure
- "No Triangulation" is a named concept, makes it memorable and shareable
- Ends with a punchy one-liner that reframes the whole thing

## Caption 4: Founder Journey / Stage Walkthrough

**Angle:** Universal truth, stage-by-stage breakdown, identity shift
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn/Instagram, entrepreneurship

```
Every founder thinks this about their own scale-up journey:

It will be different from everyone else's.

It'll be smoother, easier, less painful, whatever.

But guess what?

It won't be.

Here's what actually happens:

- You start with everything feeling possible, and that's when you hit the first real test: does anybody care about this?

- If they do, you get lift-off. One ICP, one problem, one route to market. And it works, for a while.

- Then you hit validation. This is the most dangerous stage because it doesn't feel dangerous.

Revenue's growing. You've got customers. You think you've made it but...

You haven't.

Next comes No Man's Land.

Profits drop, your growth stalls, and you're working harder than ever and getting less done.

Too small to scale properly, too big to stay scrappy.

Most businesses die here, and it isn't because the product's wrong.

It's because the founder can't make the shift from hero to architect.

Some founders never make it past No Man's Land, but if you know what's coming, you can prepare for it.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens by naming something every founder secretly believes, then immediately deflates it
- Walks through stages using bullet points, building tension at each one
- "No Man's Land" is named and defined, becomes a concept people identify with
- The real insight is buried in the middle: "it doesn't feel dangerous" is the warning
- Ends on the shift from "hero to architect" which is the identity-level takeaway

## Caption 5: Micro Case Study / Inspiration

**Angle:** Specific numbers, aspirational outcome, simplicity
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn/Instagram/X, universal

```
This is crazy.

I met an entrepreneur who was a UX designer.

He ran an online discussion group with a few thousand members and noticed that many sought his feedback on their work.

He launched a UX Feedback Membership:

£49 per month.

Two 1-hour live Q&A sessions per week.

Submit your work and receive a critique.

He grew it to 235 members paying £49 per month, generating £138K per year, with a Zoom account as his main overhead.

Now, he can live and work from anywhere while putting in just a few hours per week.

We live in remarkable times.

Don't let anyone tell you otherwise.
```

**What makes it work:**
- "This is crazy" is a simple, high-energy hook
- Real numbers (235 members, £49/mo, £138K/year) make it concrete and believable
- The simplicity of the business model is the point, Zoom as main overhead
- Short sentences walk through the progression like building blocks
- Ends with a universal, optimistic truth that wraps the specific story

## Caption 6: Reassurance / Data Reframe

**Angle:** Counter-anxiety, perspective shift, celebration
**Platform tone:** LinkedIn/X, tech/AI audience

```
Important reminder.

You're not falling behind on AI.

Each dot represents 3.2M people and most haven't ever touched AI.

Only the tiniest number pay $20 per month and only a fraction of those use it or use it for coding.

If you start using AI to build something, you are at the very pointy end of the wedge. Celebrate that.
```

**What makes it work:**
- Opens by naming the anxiety the reader already has
- Uses a visual reference (dots) to reframe their position
- Specific data makes the reassurance credible, not just feel-good
- "Pointy end of the wedge" is vivid and memorable
- Short and punchy. Doesn't overstay its welcome.
