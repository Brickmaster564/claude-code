# Universal Swipe File DNA

Structural patterns and rules extracted from proven winning ad copy. Apply these across all verticals and channels.

**Source:** Jasper's live campaign ads across Client Network verticals.

---

## Voice DNA — How Winning Copy Sounds

These ads do NOT sound like a copywriter wrote them. They sound like a knowledgeable friend giving advice.

| Rule | What it means | What to AVOID |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Conversational, not clever** | Write like you're texting a friend who asked for help. No literary flair. | Dramatic metaphors, "imagine this..." storytelling, poetic language |
| **Soft fear, not graphic fear** | "If something happened to you" — not "If you died tomorrow." Imply, don't shock. | "Imagine your partner alone, grieving..." — too intense for cold Facebook |
| **Short sentences, short paragraphs** | 1-2 sentences per paragraph max. White space is your friend. | Dense blocks of text. Anything that looks like effort to read. |
| **Specifics early** | Lead with the offer numbers fast: coverage amount, price anchor, time to complete. | Burying the offer behind 3 paragraphs of pain agitation |
| **Helper positioning** | "We work for you, not the insurers." You're the guide, not the seller. | "Our proprietary technology..." / "We're the #1..." — self-centered positioning |
| **Facebook-native CTAs** | Reference the actual platform action: "Click 'Learn More'" — not "Act now!" | Generic marketing CTAs that don't match the platform experience |

## Structural Patterns — How Winning Ads Are Built

### Pattern 1: Hook → Pain → Offer → Bullets → CTA
The most common structure. Works across verticals.

```
[Hook — 1 line, question or statement that stops scroll]

[Pain — 1-2 short paragraphs, name the consequence of inaction]

[Offer — what you do for them, positioned as helper]

- [Benefit bullet]
- [Benefit bullet]
- [Benefit bullet]

[CTA — references platform action]
```

### Pattern 2: Hook → Identity → Offer → Bullets → CTA
Leads with who they ARE, not what they fear.

```
[Identity statement — "Being a parent means..."]

[Consequence bridge — connects identity to the problem]

[Offer — positioned as the thing that completes their responsibility]

- [Benefit bullet]
- [Benefit bullet]
- [Benefit bullet]

[CTA]
```

### Pattern 3: Short-Form Punch
3-4 lines max. Pure offer, no storytelling.

```
[Benefit headline — "Affordable [Product] For [Audience]!"]

[Qualifier + offer in one sentence]

[CTA — "Get a free quote now."]
```

### Pattern 4: Objection Killer
Names the objection, destroys it with a stat or reframe, then offers.

```
[Fear hook]

[Pain consequence — 1-2 lines]

[Objection named — "90% don't have it because they think it's too expensive."]

[Reframe — "You can get [big number] for as little as [tiny price]."]

[CTA]
```

### Pattern 5: Urgency / Timeliness
Ties the action to a moment (new year, life event, season).

```
[Time anchor — "Make 2026 the year you finally..."]

[Qualifier — age/situation filter]

[Offer with price anchor]

[CTA with speed proof — "Takes two minutes."]
```

## Hook DNA — What Makes a Hook Work

From proven winners, hooks follow these rules:

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Question > Statement** for cold traffic | "If something happened to you, who would pay the mortgage?" |
| **Consequence-first** — name what goes wrong | "They'd struggle with the mortgage, bills, and daily expenses..." |
| **Identity-first** — name who they are | "Being a parent means your life is no longer just your own." |
| **Task-first** — frame it as a to-do | "Cross life insurance off the list." |
| **Time-first** — tie to a moment | "Make 2026 the year you finally set up a safety net." |

**Hook length:** 1 line. Max 2. If your hook needs a paragraph, it's not a hook.

## Price Anchoring DNA

Never state the price as a number per month. Always compare to something trivially cheap:

| Do this | Not this |
|---------|---------|
| "as little as a coffee per day" | "$19/month" |
| "less than coffee money per month" | "from $0.63/day" |
| "a cup of coffee a day" | "affordable rates" |

The coffee comparison works because it's:
- Universal (everyone buys coffee)
- Trivial (no one thinks twice about coffee money)
- Visual (they can picture it)

---

## Benefit Bullet DNA

Bullets are scannable proof points. Structure:

| Format | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Bold label + detail** | **High Coverage:** Get up to $2M to replace your income |
| **Simple dash list** | - Coverage up to $2M |
| **Checkmark prefix** | Free quote in minutes |

Rules:
- Max 3-4 bullets per ad
- Each bullet kills one objection (cost, time, complexity, trust)
- Lead with the biggest number or most surprising benefit

## CTA DNA

CTAs must match the platform and the friction level:

| Platform | CTA style |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meta ads** | "Click 'Learn More' to [action]." — references the actual button |
| **Google** | Direct benefit in headline, CTA implicit |
| **Landing page** | Button text = verb + what they get: "Get My Free Quote" |
| **Email** | Conversational: "Hit reply" or "Click here to see your quote" |

CTA rules:
- Tell them exactly what happens next
- Keep friction language low: "free," "no obligation," "takes two minutes"
- End the ad with the CTA, never with a question

## What Winning Copy Does NOT Do

These are patterns from the proven ads that are deliberately ABSENT:

| Absent pattern | Why it's missing |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Long storytelling openers | Cold Facebook traffic won't read 3 paragraphs before the point |
| "Imagine..." scenarios | Too dramatic for the market. Soft implication works better. |
| Competitor call-outs by name | Positioned as helper, not attacker |
| Urgency countdown / scarcity | No fake deadlines. Real urgency comes from life events + price anchoring |
| Clever wordplay / puns | Sounds like a copywriter, not a friend |
| Heavy statistics | One stat max ("90% don't have it"). Rest is benefit-driven. |
| "I" / first-person confessional | Ads speak TO the prospect, not FROM a narrator |
| Emoji-heavy formatting | Minimal or none. Clean, readable. |

## Applying This DNA to New Verticals

When writing for a new vertical, map these elements:

1. **Hook:** What's the consequence question? ("If [bad thing], who would [handle consequence]?")
2. **Pain bridge:** What's the 1-2 line consequence expansion?
3. **Objection killer:** What's the #1 reason they haven't acted? Name it, stat it, reframe it.
4. **Price anchor:** What's the "coffee per day" equivalent for this vertical?
5. **Identity hook:** What role/identity makes this their responsibility? ("Being a [role] means...")
6. **Helper position:** How do you position as guide, not seller? ("We work for you, not the [industry].")
7. **Bullets:** What are the 3-4 objection-killing proof points?
8. **CTA:** What's the lowest-friction next step?
