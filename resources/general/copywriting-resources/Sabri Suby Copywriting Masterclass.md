	•	Core Philosophy
The foundational beliefs that underpin Sabri Suby's approach to copywriting are rooted entirely in direct response marketing — a discipline where every word must earn its place and drive a measurable, speciﬁc action.
Direct Response vs. Branded Copy. Copywriting splits into two schools. Branded copywriting produces jingles, slogans, and emotional brand associations. Direct response copywriting writes black words on a white page and moves someone emotionally to the point where they take a speciﬁc action — becoming a lead or pulling out their credit card. Everything in this framework is direct response.
Salesmanship Multiplied. Copywriting is the highest-leverage skill in business. Suby began his career making 600 cold calls a day from a shipping container in Byron Bay. He

discovered that the words he said, and the sequence in which he said them, completely determined his results. The transition from one-to-one selling to writing ads was simply taking those same principles and multiplying them: instead of reaching 600 people a day, his ads reached 600,000. Your copy is an army of little salespeople deployed at scale, whose sole job is to capture money and bring it home.
Human Psychology is Timeless. Technology changes. Platforms come and go. But the fundamental drivers of human behaviour — pains, fears, hopes, and dreams — have not changed in 500 years. The same principles that worked in Victorian-era direct mail work in Facebook ads today. Master the psychology and you can apply it to any platform, any format, at any point in history.
Everything is Copy. The spoken word, the video script, the email, the ad, the landing page
— it all starts with the word and the message. Making a clear and compelling message that gets people to do what you want them to do is the universal skill that underpins all of it.
Clarity Over Cleverness. The goal is never to impress. It is to be understood. You will never alienate a prospect by making your message too clear, but you will lose them by making it too confusing. Your writing should be invisible — the reader should not notice the craft; they should only feel the message.
"Consumption precedes conversion. The only way to get someone to convert is to get them to consume your message."


	•	The Halo Strategy — Forum Foraging
The Halo Strategy is a systematic research methodology for discovering what to say to your market. It is founded on the principle articulated by Robert Collier: the most eﬀective copy enters the conversation already taking place in the prospect's mind.
"You don't want to create desire. You want to channel desire."

The Core Principle
Most copywriters sit down with a blank page and try to invent compelling language. The Halo Strategy inverts this. You go out into the world, ﬁnd the exact language your prospects are already using, and then assemble that language into a framework. The result is copy that sounds like it was written by one of them — because it was.
When a prospect reads your copy and thinks "this person knows exactly how I feel — they must have the answer," you have won.

Building the Pain/Fear/Hope/Dream Document
As you conduct your research, create a central document and copy and paste the exact phrases your prospects use. Organise them into four categories:
	•	Pains — What are their current frustrations and problems? What is keeping them up at night?
	•	Fears — What are their anxieties about the future? What are they afraid will happen?
	•	Hopes — What do they wish they could achieve? What would make them feel better?
	•	Dreams — What is the ultimate outcome they desire? What does their best-case life look like?
This document becomes the raw material for all your copy. You are not writing — you are assembling. You take the pains and fears you have already observed and arrange them into a framework. The language is theirs, not yours.

Frequency of Research
This is not a once-and-done exercise. Markets shift, new competitors enter, technology changes, and the conversation evolves. You cannot go more than six months without repeating this exercise. The moment your copy starts to feel stale, return to the forums.


	•	Research Over Writing
The single most important hierarchy in copywriting is this: what you say is inﬁnitely more important than how you say it.

The Bullseye Concept
In every market there is a bullseye — a speciﬁc message, angle, or idea that, if you hit it, causes prospects to buy emotionally, irrationally, and in large quantities. Finding that bullseye is the entire game. The most successful marketing campaigns in history were built on a copywriter who was obsessed with research, not with their own writing ability.
"You can't have a shitty message written in the best way. You can't out-write bad research."

The Discovery Process
You ﬁnd the bullseye by testing multiple diﬀerent angles simultaneously. Run several ads with diﬀerent core messages — diﬀerent angles on the pain, diﬀerent promises, diﬀerent framings. One of them will produce an outlier result: a signiﬁcantly lower cost per lead, a higher click-through rate, or a better conversion rate. That outlier is your "what to say." Only once you have identiﬁed the winning message do you then invest heavily in optimising how you say it.


	•	The Prospect-First Principle
Eﬀective copy is never about the company, its products, or its history. It is always, entirely, about the prospect.

The 80/20 Rule
Apply this test to every piece of copy you write: is 80% of this talking about the prospect
— their pain, their desire, the beneﬁt they want — and only 10-20% about you or how you deliver the outcome? If the ratio is inverted, the copy will fail.
A telltale sign of bad copy is counting the words "and," "we," and "I" versus the word "you."

If the former outnumber the latter, the copy is self-centred and will not convert.

Features vs. Beneﬁts
Features tell, beneﬁts sell. Prospects do not care about the mechanics of your product. They care about the end result it will deliver for them.

Feature

"12-week programme with macro calculations"
Beneﬁt

"Drop two dress sizes and feel the best you have in years"

"Streamlined online application process"
"Get fully covered in under 15 minutes, from your sofa, without a single phone call"

"Access to 40+ insurance providers"
"We ﬁnd the cheapest policy available for your exact situation so you never overpay"


Always ask: so what does this mean for the prospect? Keep asking until you reach the emotional, tangible outcome.

"Under the Fingernail" Copy
This is one of the most powerful shifts in Suby's approach. When he focused all his eﬀorts on describing the pain his prospect was experiencing in vivid, speciﬁc, undeniable detail — and then focused on the big beneﬁt they wanted more than anything — results went "crazy."
The technique is to describe the pain so precisely that the prospect cannot deny it. You are not gently acknowledging their problem; you are pressing on the bruised knee, pouring vinegar in the wound. You agitate the pain they already have because people are more motivated to move away from pain than they are to move towards pleasure.
The pain description must be so speciﬁc that the prospect thinks: "This person has been inside my head. They understand my situation better than I can articulate it myself."

The Red-Hot Pitch Method
Sit down with your best salesperson and record them delivering a live, high-energy, 30-40 minute sales pitch. This pitch should cover all the questions, concerns, pains, fears, hopes, and dreams of the prospect, and then transition into a compelling close.
Transcribe this pitch. It is the gold standard for your messaging. Now look at your landing pages and ads. If they are saying something diﬀerent to what that red-hot pitch says, delete them. Your ads and landing pages must be as close to that pitch as possible — because that pitch is what actually converts.



	•	Headline Essentials
David Ogilvy's rule: on average, ﬁve times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. Once you have written your headline, you have already spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar.

The Norah Hayden Story
In 1982, Norah Hayden wrote a book called Astrological Love. It bombed, selling approximately 2,000 copies. A New York publisher bought the rights, did not change a single word inside the book, and simply changed the title to How to Satisfy a Woman Every Time and Have Her Beg for More. It sold 2.3 million copies in 18 months and became a New York Times bestseller. Same content. Diﬀerent packaging. The headline is everything.

Where to Study Headlines
Follow the money and the attention. The best headline writers in the world work at TMZ, the New York Times, Lad Bible, and social media gossip sites. Study how they construct their headlines. They use:
	•	Breaking news framing
	•	Words like: exposed, shocked, leaked, wild details emerge
	•	Numbers in every headline
	•	Shocking or unexpected images
	•	Three to four gripping words that elicit an emotion in the ﬁrst three seconds

The 5 Headline Essentials
	•	Grab Attention. The headline does not need to tell the full story. It needs a "grabber" — three to four words that demand attention and interrupt the scroll.
	•	Always Use Numbers. Numbers give prospects a tangible object to wrap their mind around. They also create an open loop (the brain wants to close it). "The seven things no ﬁnancial planner would dare tell you" outperforms "what you must know about ﬁnancial planners" because the number creates a speciﬁc, closeable loop.
	•	Create Burning Curiosity. Use buzzwords: shocking, alarming, breaking news. Add a twist or qualiﬁer: "seven things no property investor would dare tell you." Address the biggest objection: "how to start an online business even if you're a complete beginner." Curiosity is the biggest driver of clicks — but it must be speciﬁc curiosity paired with a big beneﬁt, not blind teaser copy.

	•	Show What's In It For Them. Every headline needs a big, speciﬁc beneﬁt. Promise something the prospect would crawl across broken glass to get. Catch more ﬂies with honey than vinegar.
	•	Make It Speciﬁc. You cannot say "grow your business." You must say "here's how to get 150 to 250 highly qualiﬁed leads each and every month on autopilot." The more jaded and advertised-to the market, the deeper the speciﬁcity required.

	•	Bullet Copy / Fascinations
Fascinations are short, punchy, intriguing bullets that tease a speciﬁc piece of information without fully revealing it. They are one of the most-read parts of any sales message because they are scannable.

How Prospects Read Sales Pages
Understanding the reading pattern is essential for placing fascinations correctly. Prospects typically:
	•	Read the headline and sub-headline
	•	Scroll to the bottom to check the price and CTA
	•	Scroll back up and read the sub-headlines

	•	Spend a disproportionate amount of time on bullet copy
This means your fascinations must be compelling enough to stop the scroll and pull the prospect into the body copy.

What Makes a Great Fascination
Each fascination must be:
	•	Hyper-speciﬁc to an exact pain point the prospect would pay money to solve
	•	Intriguing enough that the prospect feels compelled to ﬁnd out the answer
	•	Capable of justifying the purchase on its own — if every bullet were the only thing on the page, would it be enough to make someone want to take action?

	•	Overcoming Skepticism
Skepticism is at an all-time high. Barriers to entry in most industries are lower than ever, which means prospects have bought from unqualiﬁed businesses and had bad experiences. Your copy must acknowledge and dismantle this skepticism directly.

Sell Against Alternatives
It is more important to sell against all the other alternatives than simply to sell what you have. Your prospect has tried other solutions. They have used comparison sites, gone direct to insurers, or simply done nothing. Your copy must:
	•	Acknowledge the alternatives they have tried or are considering
	•	Explain precisely why those alternatives failed or are inadequate
	•	Position your solution as categorically diﬀerent
This is "throwing stones at competitors" — not by naming them directly, but by dismantling the category of solution they represent.

The Antidote is Value
The single most powerful way to overcome skepticism is to provide genuine value before asking for anything in return. Your ads and funnels must leave the prospect better than you found them. Make your free content — a guide, a quiz, a report — better than your competitors' paid products. When you prove you can help people by actually helping them, the skepticism dissolves.

Handle Objections in the Copy
A buyer reads all the copy. They are looking for answers to their objections and concerns. If your copy is compelling but people are still not converting, the problem is not the oﬀer — it is that they do not believe you. You must identify every objection your prospect has and address it explicitly within the copy, before they have the chance to raise it.
"After writing your sales message, it should sound so compelling that only a lunatic wouldn't buy it. If it sounds that good and people still aren't buying, the answer is they

don't believe you."

The Buyer Principle
A buyer is a buyer is a buyer. The people who buy from you are also buying from your competitors. Understand everything they are buying in your category so you can sell against those things. Your copy must make the case not just for your product, but against every alternative they might consider instead.


	•	Long Form vs. Short Form
This is a settled debate. Long-form copy outperforms short-form copy, with one critical caveat.

Why Long Form Wins
A person who is genuinely in the market to solve their problem will consume all the information they need to make a conﬁdent decision. They want every objection handled, every question answered, every concern addressed. Your copy needs to replicate the 40-minute red-hot sales pitch. You cannot do that in three paragraphs.
The people who will not read long copy are also not going to buy. If someone will not spend their time reading your message, they will not spend their money on your product. The goal is ﬁrst to get them to spend their attention, and then to get them to spend their money.
"Somebody who's not going to read long copy is also not going to read short copy. A buyer is the person who reads all the copy."

The Critical Caveat: Every Word Must Fight
Copy cannot be long for the sake of being long. Every single word must ﬁght for its right to be on the page. Write the full draft, then edit ruthlessly. The copy must be so interesting that it feels like entertainment, not a task. If it is boring, it is too long — even if it is only one paragraph.
The standard to hold yourself to: would a reader rather keep reading than stop? If not, cut.


	•	The 17 Fastest Sales-Producing Secrets
This is the complete structural framework for a sales message, applicable to landing pages, video sales letters, long-form ads, and quiz funnel result pages.

Step 1: Call Out Your Audience
Use "eyebrow copy" — a short line above the headline that directly addresses the speciﬁc person you are writing for. This acts as a ﬁlter, ensuring the right people self-select into reading the message.
"Attention property investors who want to become millionaires."

Step 2: Demand Their Attention
Come out swinging with a big, bold, speciﬁc beneﬁt in the headline. Promise something the prospect would crawl across broken glass to get. This is not the place for subtlety.

Step 3: Back Up Your Promise in the Sub-Headline
The sub-headline expands on the headline's promise with a straightforward explanation that gives the prospect a reason to keep reading. It bridges the headline to the body copy.

Step 4: Create Irresistible Intrigue
In the leading copy below the sub-headline, fan the ﬂames of desire and curiosity. Use fascination bullets to crank up the prospect's need to know more. Open loops that can only be closed by continuing to read.

Step 5: Shine a Floodlight on the Pain
Describe their problem in vivid, intimate detail. You must demonstrate that you understand their situation so completely that they feel you have been inside their head. Kick the bruised knee. Pour vinegar in the wound. Agitate the pain they already have. People are more motivated to move away from pain than towards pleasure — use this.

Step 6: Provide a Unique Solution
Introduce your solution as categorically diﬀerent from everything they have tried before. List out the previous solutions they have attempted. Dismantle the selling arguments of each one. Show precisely why those solutions failed. Then position your mechanism as the new, diﬀerent approach that addresses the root cause.

Step 7: Show Your Credentials
Put forward your case. This is not a place for modesty. Use case studies, testimonials, video testimonials, statistics, data, and any other evidence that demonstrates you can deliver the promised outcome. Establish authority clearly.

Step 8: Detail the Beneﬁts

List all the features of your product or service. For each feature, write two corresponding beneﬁts. Then remove the features entirely and talk only about the beneﬁts. Describe speciﬁcally how the prospect's life becomes better after they buy.

Step 9: Social Proof
Show, do not tell. The number of people who have bought, reviews, speciﬁc outcomes achieved, statistics, video testimonials, screenshots of messages, bank accounts, invoices, before-and-after results. Social proof must be concrete and speciﬁc, not vague.

Step 10: Make Your Irresistible Oﬀer
State clearly and speciﬁcally what the prospect is going to get. The oﬀer must be value-packed and must clearly articulate how it achieves the desired end outcome. Ambiguity kills conversions.

Step 11: Add Sweeteners
Add 3-4 bonuses that your prospect genuinely wants. Each bonus should be valuable enough to justify the purchase price on its own. The bonuses should complement the core oﬀer and address secondary desires or concerns.

Step 12: Stack the Value
Build a table that itemises the core oﬀer and every bonus, with a monetary value assigned to each. Add them up to arrive at the highest theoretical price point. This anchors the prospect's perception of value before you reveal the actual price.

Step 13: Reveal Your Price
Position the actual price as a fraction of the stacked value. The contrast between the stacked value and the real price makes the oﬀer feel like an obvious decision.

Step 14: Inject Scarcity
Give a real, logical, believable reason why the prospect must act now. How many clients can you realistically service? Articulate that clearly. Artiﬁcial scarcity is transparent and damages trust; real scarcity (limited capacity, limited-time oﬀer with a genuine reason) creates urgency.

Step 15: Give a Powerful Guarantee
Remove all risk from the prospect and burden it entirely on yourself. The cost of acquiring customers without a guarantee is far higher than the cost of any guarantee claims. A strong

guarantee removes the ﬁnal barrier to action.

Step 16: Call to Action
Do not ask — tell. Use an embedded command. Be explicit and walk the prospect through exactly what to do next, step by step.
"Click the button below, ﬁll out your details on the next page, and you'll be taken straight to your personalised quote."

Step 17: Close with a P.S.
The P.S. is one of the most-read parts of any sales letter. Many prospects will scroll directly to the P.S. before reading anything else. Use it to restate the core beneﬁt and the pain of inaction. The formula: start with the negative consequence of not acting, then end with the positive outcome of taking action.
"P.S. — Or don't take the next step and continue worrying about what would happen to your family if the worst came to the worst. Or simply click the link below and get the peace of mind of knowing they are fully protected, starting today."


	•	Sixth Grade Reading Level
The most successful communicators — New York Times bestselling authors, the biggest YouTubers, leaders appointed to the most powerful oﬃces — write and speak at a sixth grade reading level or below.
Why This Matters. You will not alienate anyone by making your message too clear. You will lose people by making it too complex. Your job is to speak to the lowest common denominator of your market. You want the reader to be moved by your message, not to notice your writing.
The Hemingway App. This free tool analyses your copy and shows you the Flesch-Kincaid reading level. It highlights sentences that are diﬃcult to read, overly complex, or use passive voice. The goal is to have nothing ﬂagged. Get the reading level to Grade 6 or below before publishing any copy.
Practical Rules:
	•	Use simple, common words. If there is a simpler word, use it.
	•	Write short sentences. Four to ﬁve words, then a full stop and a line break.
	•	One idea per sentence.
	•	One idea per paragraph.

	•	Avoid jargon, industry terms, and acronyms unless you immediately deﬁne them.
	•	Clarity beats cleverness every single time.
