# 1. The Senior Care Industry

The senior care industry encompasses products, services, and support systems designed to help aging adults live safely and comfortably — whether in their own homes, assisted living communities, memory care facilities, or nursing homes.

Core functions include:
- In-home personal care (bathing, dressing, meal prep, medication reminders)
- In-home companion care (socialization, transportation, light housekeeping)
- Skilled home health care (nursing, physical therapy, wound care)
- Assisted living residential communities
- Memory care for Alzheimer's and dementia patients
- Adult day care programs
- Nursing home and skilled nursing facilities
- Care coordination, placement, and referral services

The industry spans two primary models:
- **Non-medical home care** — Caregivers assist with activities of daily living (ADLs) and companionship in the client's home. No medical license required in most states. This is the primary lead-gen opportunity.
- **Medical home health care** — Licensed nurses and therapists provide skilled medical care at home, typically ordered by a physician and often covered by Medicare/Medicaid.

Global elderly care market:
- **2025:** $53.3 billion
- **2034 (projected):** $114.6 billion
- **CAGR:** 8.9%

North America accounts for approximately 33% of the global elderly care market.

Demand drivers:
- **Peak 65:** ~11,400 Americans turn 65 every day in 2025 — the highest rate in history
- By 2030, all 73 million baby boomers will be 65+, making up over 20% of the U.S. population
- The oldest boomers turn 80 in 2026, dramatically increasing demand for care services
- 90% of seniors want to age in place rather than move to a facility
- Chronic caregiver shortage — demand outpaces supply of qualified caregivers
- Rising chronic disease prevalence (Alzheimer's, diabetes, heart disease) among aging population

# 3. Types of Senior Care Services

## Non-Medical Home Care (Primary Lead-Gen Target)

Caregivers provide personal care (bathing, dressing, grooming), companionship, meal preparation, transportation, light housekeeping, and medication reminders. No medical license required. Franchise-dominated model. Examples: Visiting Angels, Home Instead, Comfort Keepers, Right at Home.

## Home Health Care (Medical)

Licensed nurses and therapists deliver skilled medical care at home — wound care, IV therapy, physical/occupational therapy, post-surgical recovery. Typically physician-ordered and Medicare-eligible. Examples: Amedisys, LHC Group, BrightSpring Health.

## Assisted Living

Residential communities where seniors live in private apartments with access to meals, personal care assistance, medication management, housekeeping, and social activities. 24-hour staff on site. Median cost: $6,200/month. Examples: Brookdale, Sunrise Senior Living, Five Star Senior Living.

## Memory Care

Specialized, secure environments with 24/7 support and structured routines designed specifically for Alzheimer's and dementia patients. Staff receive additional training in dementia care. Often a wing or unit within an assisted living community. Higher cost than standard assisted living.

## Nursing Home / Skilled Nursing Facility

Highest level of care outside a hospital. 24-hour skilled nursing. For seniors with complex medical needs. Median cost: $9,800–$11,300/month. Often covered partially by Medicare/Medicaid for qualifying stays.

## Adult Day Care

Daytime programs offering activities, meals, socialization, and some personal care assistance. Allows family caregivers to work or take respite. Median cost: ~$1,700/month.

## Senior Care Referral / Placement Services

Platforms and advisors that help families find appropriate care options. Free to families; compensated by providers on referral. Examples: A Place for Mom, Caring.com, CarePatrol.

# 4. Key Terminology

## Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)
Basic self-care tasks: bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring (moving from bed to chair), and continence. The primary measure of whether someone needs care.

## Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADLs)
More complex tasks: managing finances, medication management, transportation, meal preparation, housekeeping, shopping. Decline in IADLs often precedes ADL decline.

## Aging in Place
Living in one's own home safely and independently for as long as possible, rather than moving to a care facility.

## Respite Care
Temporary, short-term care that gives family caregivers a break. Can be in-home or facility-based.

## Long-Term Care Insurance (LTCI)
Insurance that covers costs of long-term care services not covered by regular health insurance or Medicare. Only ~7% of adults over 50 have it.

## Medicare vs. Medicaid
- **Medicare:** Federal health insurance for 65+. Covers skilled home health care (short-term, medically necessary). Does NOT cover non-medical home care or assisted living.
- **Medicaid:** Federal-state program for low-income individuals. Covers nursing home care and, in many states, home and community-based services (HCBS waivers).

## Caregiver
A person who provides care — either a family member (informal/unpaid caregiver) or a professional (paid caregiver/home care aide).

## Home Care Aide / Personal Care Aide
A non-medical caregiver who assists with ADLs, companionship, and household tasks.

## Certified Nursing Assistant (CNA)
A trained and certified individual who provides basic nursing care under the supervision of a nurse.

# 5. The Lead Buyers

Primary buyers seek adult children (ages 40–65) who are researching care options for an aging parent — ideally with financial resources to pay privately or through long-term care insurance.

## Categories

### National Home Care Franchise Networks

The largest buyers of senior care leads. Franchise owners in local territories purchase leads to grow their client base.

Examples:
- **Visiting Angels** — 600+ franchise locations across all 50 states. Largest non-medical home care franchise.
- **Home Instead** — 1,200+ franchise locations worldwide. Specializes in Alzheimer's and dementia care.
- **Comfort Keepers** — 700+ locations. Personal care and companionship.
- **Right at Home** — 700+ locations. Non-medical and skilled care.
- **BrightStar Care** — 380+ locations. Full continuum from companion to skilled nursing.
- **Senior Helpers** — 300+ locations. Specialized Parkinson's and dementia care.

### Independent / Regional Home Care Agencies

Local agencies building their client census. Often buy leads from aggregators or invest in local SEO/PPC.

### Assisted Living Communities

Facilities seeking to fill rooms. Longer sales cycle. Higher lifetime value per resident ($75K–$90K+/year in revenue).

### Senior Care Referral Platforms

Companies that aggregate demand and sell referrals to care providers.

Examples:
- **A Place for Mom** — Largest senior care referral service. Free to families; paid by providers on move-in.
- **Caring.com** — Online directory and referral platform.
- **CarePatrol** — Franchise-based senior care advisory service.

### Lead Aggregators & Brokers

Companies that generate and sell leads to the above buyers.

Buyers track:
- CPL (Cost Per Lead) — Senior care leads range $18–$60+ depending on type, exclusivity, and geography
- CPA (Cost Per Acquisition) — $100–$430+
- LTV (Lifetime Value) — Home care clients average 12–24+ months of service
- Average revenue per home care client: $3,000–$6,000+/month for full-time care

# 6. Customer Demographics

## The Decision Maker (Lead Target)

The person searching for and selecting care is almost never the senior — it's their **adult child**.

- **Age:** 45–65 (primarily Gen X, increasingly older Millennials)
- **Gender:** 66% female (daughters and daughters-in-law are the primary decision-makers)
- **Average age:** 49 years old
- **Income:** Middle to upper-middle class ($75K–$200K household income)
- **Employment:** Majority are working full-time while also caregiving
- **Geography:** Nationwide, but highest demand in states with large 65+ populations (FL, CA, TX, PA, NY, OH)

## The Care Recipient

- **Age:** 70–90+ (median around 80)
- **Gender:** Predominantly female (women live longer; 31.9 million women 65+ vs. 25.9 million men)
- **Living situation:** 28% of seniors live alone; 59% live with a spouse/partner
- **Health triggers:** Dementia/Alzheimer's, mobility limitations, falls, post-hospitalization, chronic illness

## Key Purchase Triggers

- Parent had a fall or health scare
- Noticed cognitive decline (forgetting medication, getting lost, confusion)
- Parent can no longer drive safely
- Parent's spouse/primary caregiver died or became unable to provide care
- Hospital discharge with need for ongoing help at home
- Family caregiver burnout — can't keep up with work + caregiving
- Neighbor or friend expressed concern about parent's safety
- Parent living alone and family lives far away

Key cost facts:
- In-home care is cheaper than assisted living if the senior needs fewer than ~40 hours/week of care
- 70% of people turning 65 will need some form of long-term care
- Medicare does NOT cover non-medical home care (the most common type)
- Only ~7% of adults over 50 have long-term care insurance
- Most families pay out of pocket or use Medicaid after spending down assets

# 8. Regulatory Landscape

The senior care industry is regulated at federal, state, and local levels — with significant variation by state.

## State Licensing

- Each state has its own licensing requirements for home care agencies
- Some states (like California, New York, Florida) require agency licensing for non-medical home care; others do not
- State Departments of Health oversee licensing, inspections, and complaint investigations
- Assisted living regulations vary dramatically by state — no federal standards exist

## Federal Regulations

- **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services):** Regulates Medicare-certified home health agencies and nursing facilities
- **FTC:** Enforces against deceptive advertising and marketing practices
- **HIPAA:** Protects patient health information in care settings

## Advertising Restrictions

- No guarantees of specific health outcomes
- No misleading claims about caregiver qualifications or staffing ratios
- Must disclose costs and any limitations of services accurately
- Cannot imply Medicare/insurance coverage for services that aren't covered
- Must be truthful about availability and response times
- Testimonials must reflect genuine experiences

## Caregiver Requirements

- Background check requirements vary by state
- Some states require minimum training hours for home care aides
- CNAs must pass state certification exams
- Licensed home health aides (HHAs) require additional training beyond personal care aides
