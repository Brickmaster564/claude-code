## 11. Under-the-Fingernail Copy Blocks

**Block 1: The Phone Call**

It's 2pm on a Tuesday. You're at work. Your phone lights up and your stomach drops. You don't even look at the number. You just know. It's either the hospital, a neighbor, or your mother, confused, calling you for the third time today because she can't remember if she took her medication. You step into the hallway. You answer. She fell. In the kitchen. Tried to reach something on the top shelf. Nobody was there. She laid on the floor for two hours before the neighbor heard her calling out. They called 911. She's at the ER. Hip fracture. Surgery tomorrow. Your boss is asking where you went. Your kids need to be picked up in an hour. And all you can think is: I knew this was going to happen. I knew it, and I didn't do anything about it.

**Block 2: The Nightly Drive**

Every night after work, you drive 40 minutes to Mom's house. You check the stove. Count the pills. Make sure she's eaten something that isn't toast. You wash the dishes. You put away the mail she's been stacking on the counter for a week. You pretend not to notice the bruise on her arm she says is nothing. You sit with her for an hour because you know once you leave, she won't talk to another human being until you come back tomorrow. Then you drive home. Make dinner for your own family at 8:30. Help with homework. Collapse into bed. And lay there, unable to sleep, because you're already thinking about doing it all again tomorrow. Your husband says something has to give. Your kids ask why you're never home. Your boss noticed you're leaving early. You're the only sibling who shows up. And you're drowning. Not because you don't love her enough. Because you love her too much to admit you can't do this alone.

**Block 3: The Decline**

It started small. She asked the same question twice in one conversation. You laughed it off. Then she forgot your daughter's name. Just for a second. Then it was the grocery store she's been going to for 30 years, and she couldn't remember how to get home. The doctor said the word everyone dreads. And suddenly the woman who raised you, who taught you to read, who held your hand at every school play, is looking at you with eyes that aren't quite sure who you are. You Google "memory care." You see the costs. $7,500 to $8,500 a month. Your chest tightens. You look at assisted living. $6,200 a month. In-home care. $30 an hour. None of it is covered by Medicare. You close the laptop. You sit in the dark. And you feel a kind of grief you didn't know existed, for someone who is still here but slowly leaving. The hardest part is not the money. It's not the logistics. It's watching her become someone she never wanted to be, and knowing that the clock only moves in one direction. The only thing you can control is what happens between now and then. Whether she's alone, or whether someone is there.
