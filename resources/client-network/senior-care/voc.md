# Senior Care — Voice of Customer (VOC)

**Date:** March 2, 2026

---

## 1. Customer Avatar: The Senior Care Decision-Maker

The lead is almost never the senior — it's their **adult child** searching for help.

**Demographics:**
- Age 45–65 (Gen X core, older Millennials emerging)
- 66% female — daughters and daughters-in-law drive the majority of care decisions
- Average age: 49
- Household income $75K–$200K (must afford $30–$36/hour or $5,000–$7,000+/month out of pocket)
- Working full-time while also caregiving
- Concentrated in states with large 65+ populations: FL, CA, TX, PA, NY, OH
- Typically lives 30 minutes to several hours from the aging parent

**Life Situation Triggers:**
- Parent had a fall, hospitalization, or health scare
- Noticed cognitive decline — forgetting medication, confusion, getting lost
- Parent can no longer drive safely or manage daily tasks alone
- Parent's spouse (the primary caregiver) died or became too ill to continue
- Hospital discharge with doctor recommending ongoing help at home
- Siblings pressuring them to "do something" about Mom or Dad
- Got a concerned call from a neighbor, friend, or parent's doctor
- Reached personal burnout trying to juggle work, kids, and caregiving
- Parent living alone and the family lives far away

**Psychographic Profile:**
- Overwhelmed and emotionally drained — this decision has been building for months or years
- Feels guilty no matter what they choose (guilty for "putting Mom in a home," guilty for not doing enough, guilty for resenting the burden)
- Deeply loyal to their parent but stretched impossibly thin
- Wants to do the right thing but has no idea what the "right thing" is
- Terrified of making the wrong choice — this feels permanent and high-stakes
- Skeptical of the industry after hearing about neglect, scams, or overcharging
- Price-sensitive but willing to pay for quality and peace of mind
- Information-overloaded — has Googled extensively but feels more confused, not less

- "I haven't had a day off in months — I'm running on empty"
- "I feel like I'm failing at everything — my job, my kids, and taking care of my parent"
- "I'm the only sibling who steps up and I'm exhausted"
- "I can't keep doing this. Something has to give."
- "My marriage is suffering because all my energy goes to caregiving"
- "I cry in my car before I walk into work because I'm so overwhelmed"

### Fear for Parent's Safety
- "I'm terrified she's going to fall and no one will be there"
- "He left the stove on twice last week — I can't stop thinking about it"
- "She got lost driving to the grocery store she's been going to for 30 years"
- "I found expired food in the fridge and pills she hasn't been taking"
- "Every time my phone rings I think it's the hospital"
- "He's losing weight and I don't think he's eating when I'm not there"
- "She fell in the bathroom and laid there for hours before anyone found her"

### The Agonizing Decision
- "I don't know if I should bring someone into her home or move her to a facility"
- "How do I know which agency is actually good and which one is just marketing?"
- "I've read 50 reviews and I'm more confused than when I started"
- "I feel like I'm choosing between bad options"
- "I don't even know what questions to ask"
- "Everyone has an opinion — my siblings, my spouse, her doctor — and they all disagree"

### Guilt
- "She took care of me my whole life — I should be able to take care of her"
- "Am I abandoning her by hiring someone else to do this?"
- "She says she doesn't want a stranger in her house and it breaks my heart"
- "I feel guilty for wanting my life back"
- "I promised Dad I'd take care of her — am I breaking that promise?"
- "When I'm at work I feel guilty I'm not with her, when I'm with her I feel guilty I'm not with my kids"

### Financial Stress
- "I had no idea home care costs $6,000+ a month — I thought Medicare covered this"
- "We can't afford a nursing home, but I can't keep doing this myself"
- "Mom's savings are going to run out in two years at this rate"
- "Nobody in our family planned for this and now we're scrambling"
- "I'm dipping into my own retirement to pay for her care"

## 3. Fears (Deepest Emotional Drivers)

- **Neglect:** "What if the caregiver ignores her, or worse — mistreats her when no one's watching?"
- **Stranger danger:** "I'm supposed to let a stranger into my mother's home and trust them with her life?"
- **Wrong choice:** "What if I pick the wrong place or the wrong caregiver and something terrible happens?"
- **Financial ruin:** "What if her care drains every penny and there's nothing left — for her or for us?"
- **Regret:** "What if I look back and realize I should have done more, done it sooner, done it differently?"
- **Loss of identity:** "I'm watching my parent become someone I don't recognize — and I can't stop it"
- **Being judged:** "My siblings / family / friends will think I'm a bad daughter if I hire help instead of doing it myself"
- **Their own mortality:** "If this is what happens to her, what's going to happen to me?"
- **Losing time:** "She might not have much time left and I'm spending it stressed instead of present"
- **Being scammed:** "This industry feels predatory — everyone wants to charge $6,000 a month and I don't know who to trust"

## 6. Objections (Why They Haven't Taken Action Yet)

### "It's too expensive"
- Home care averages $30–$36/hour; full-time care runs $6,000–$7,000+/month
- Nursing homes cost $10,000–$11,000+/month
- Medicare doesn't cover non-medical home care — most families don't know this until they need it
- Only 7% of adults 50+ have long-term care insurance
- **Reality:** Delaying care often leads to emergency hospitalizations, falls, and crises that cost far more

### "Mom says she doesn't need help"
- Seniors resist care out of pride, fear of losing independence, or denial of decline
- "She gets angry when I bring it up"
- **Counter:** Frame it as companionship, not "care." Start with light help (housekeeping, errands) and build trust

### "I should be doing this myself"
- Cultural and family pressure to handle it without outside help
- Guilt about "outsourcing" a family responsibility
- **Reality:** 65 million Americans are caregivers. Burnout is real and leads to worse outcomes for both caregiver and parent

### "I don't trust strangers in her home"
- Fear of theft, neglect, or abuse — fueled by news stories and reviews
- Previous bad experience with a caregiver or agency
- **Counter:** Reputable agencies run background checks, bond/insure caregivers, provide supervision, and allow caregiver matching/replacement

### "I don't know where to start"
- Overwhelmed by options — home care vs. assisted living vs. memory care vs. nursing home
- Hundreds of agencies in any given metro area
- Conflicting information online
- **Counter:** Free consultations, care assessments, and referral services exist to simplify the process

### "We're not there yet / it's not that bad"
- Denial or underestimation of decline
- Waiting for a crisis rather than planning proactively
- **Reality:** 70% of people turning 65 will need long-term care. Starting early leads to better outcomes and lower costs

### "I've heard horror stories"
- News coverage of nursing home neglect, caregiver abuse, billing scams
- Online reviews featuring worst-case scenarios
- **Counter:** Quality providers welcome transparency — ask about training, supervision, client references, and complaint history

## 8. Emotional Journey Map

### Stage 1: Denial / "We're Fine"
"Mom's just getting older — it's normal. She doesn't need help."
- Doesn't see the signs, or minimizes them. Parent covers up struggles. Family assumes everything is fine.
- Emotional state: oblivious, occasionally concerned but reassured.

### Stage 2: Trigger Event
"Oh God — something is really wrong."
- A fall. A hospitalization. Leaving the stove on. Getting lost. Forgetting names. A concerned call from a neighbor or doctor.
- Emotional state: shock, fear, urgency, "I should have seen this sooner."

### Stage 3: Frantic Research / Overwhelm
"What do I do? Where do I even start?"
- Googles "home care for elderly parent." Reads reviews. Calls agencies. Asks friends. Gets conflicting advice. Feels paralyzed by options and terrified of making the wrong choice.
- Emotional state: overwhelmed, anxious, guilty, information-overloaded, decision fatigue.

### Stage 4: Decision / Relief
"We finally have someone — and Mom actually likes her."
- Hires a caregiver or places parent. First week is nerve-wracking. Then the caregiver builds rapport. Updates start coming. Parent seems happier. The weight begins to lift.
- Emotional state: relief, cautious optimism, gratitude, "I should have done this sooner."

## 9. Channel-Specific Language Patterns

### Facebook / Instagram (Interruption — awareness stage)
- Lead with caregiver burnout or parent safety: "Still checking on Mom every night after work? There's a better way."
- Use emotional resonance: "You promised you'd take care of her. That doesn't mean you have to do it alone."
- Visual: candid family moments, daughter helping elderly mother, warm in-home scenes
- Target: Women 40–65, interests in caregiving, AARP, senior living, sandwich generation

### Google Search (Intent — consideration/decision stage)
- Match high-intent queries: "in-home care for elderly parent near me," "how much does home care cost," "home care vs assisted living"
- Lead with solution + differentiator: "Trusted In-Home Caregivers. Background-Checked. Matched to Your Parent. Free Consultation."
- Address the cost question head-on — it's the #1 search intent

### YouTube Pre-Roll (Interruption — awareness/consideration)
- Open with the emotional moment: "When was the last time you worried about your parent living alone?"
- Transition to relief: show a caregiver arriving, a family breathing easier, a parent smiling
- CTA: free care assessment or consultation

## 10. Halo Strategy Language (Sabri Suby Framework)

### What they SAY (surface level)
- "I'm looking for a caregiver for my mom"
- "How much does home care cost?"
- "What's the best home care agency near me?"
- "Is assisted living better than home care?"
- "Does Medicare cover in-home care?"

### What they THINK (consideration level)
- "I don't want to overpay or get scammed"
- "I need someone I can actually trust in her home"
- "What if the caregiver doesn't show up or doesn't care?"
- "I can't figure out what Mom actually needs — companion care? Personal care? Nursing?"
- "I don't know how we're going to afford this long-term"

### What they FEEL (emotional level)
- Guilt for not being able to do it all themselves
- Fear that something terrible will happen to their parent
- Exhaustion from carrying the weight of caregiving alone
- Grief — watching their parent decline and lose independence
- Resentment they're afraid to admit — toward siblings who don't help, toward the situation itself

### What they truly DESIRE (identity level)
- To be a good son or daughter — to honor the person who raised them
- To give their parent dignity, comfort, and safety in their final chapter
- To stop living in constant fear and guilt
- To get their own life back without feeling like a terrible person for wanting it
- To know, when the time comes, that they did everything they could
