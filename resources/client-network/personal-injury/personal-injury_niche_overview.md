# 1. The Personal Injury Industry

Personal injury (PI) law encompasses civil cases where an individual suffers physical, emotional, or financial harm due to another party's negligence, recklessness, or intentional misconduct. The practice area covers a broad spectrum of incidents, from car crashes and slip-and-fall accidents to medical malpractice and defective products, with the unifying principle being that the injured party (plaintiff) seeks monetary compensation from the responsible party (defendant) or their insurer. Attorneys in this field almost universally work on a contingency fee basis, meaning clients pay nothing upfront and the lawyer collects a percentage of the settlement or verdict only if the case is won.

Motor vehicle accidents (MVAs) are the dominant sub-category within personal injury law, accounting for more than 52% of all PI case filings nationwide [1]. This makes MVA the single largest revenue driver for the industry and the most natural entry point for pay-per-lead (PPL) operations. The sheer volume of accidents, combined with the near-universal need for legal representation in injury claims, creates a massive and recurring demand pipeline. Every day in the United States, more than 6,500 people are injured in motor vehicle crashes [2], and the vast majority of those individuals will need to navigate insurance claims, medical bills, and potential litigation, often for the first time in their lives.

The PI industry is highly fragmented, with no single firm holding more than 5% market share [3]. It is dominated by solo practitioners and small firms, many of whom rely heavily on external lead generation to maintain their caseloads. This fragmentation, combined with the contingency fee model (which makes high case values extremely lucrative), creates intense competition for new clients and makes PI one of the most expensive verticals in digital advertising.

# 2. Market Size and Trends

## Industry Revenue

The US personal injury lawyers and attorneys industry generated an estimated **$61.7 billion in revenue in 2025**, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 2.5% over the preceding five years [3]. Industry revenue is projected to exceed **$63 billion in 2026** as case volumes and settlement values continue their upward trend [1]. The broader economic cost of unintentional injuries in the US reached **$1.33 trillion in 2023** when accounting for medical expenses, lost productivity, and quality-of-life impacts [2].

## Number of Attorneys and Firms

Approximately **135,000 to 165,000 personal injury attorneys** practice in the United States, representing roughly 10% of the nation's 1.3 million licensed lawyers [2][4]. These attorneys are spread across an estimated **50,000 to 60,000 law firms**, the overwhelming majority of which are small operations with fewer than 10 attorneys [3]. The market is extraordinarily fragmented, with no company holding market share exceeding 5% [3].

## Annual Case Volume

Nearly **400,000 personal injury claims** are filed annually in US state courts, with some estimates ranging between 300,000 and 500,000 depending on how filings are counted [2][5]. Beyond formal filings, an estimated **39.5 million personal injury incidents** per year require medical treatment, representing the broader pool of potential cases [1]. The vast majority of formal claims settle before trial: approximately **95-96% of PI cases resolve through pre-trial settlement**, with only 4-5% ever reaching a courtroom [1][5].

## Settlement Values

Average settlement amounts vary significantly by case type and injury severity, but broad benchmarks include:

- **Overall PI average settlement:** $24,000 to $55,000 (median around $31,000) [6]
- **Average federal court PI damages:** $75,000 [2]
- **Auto liability bodily injury claims (2024):** $27,373 average, reflecting an 8% year-over-year increase [2]

## Advertising Spend

Legal services advertising in the United States exceeded **$2.5 billion in 2024**, spread across more than 26.9 million ad placements [7]. Of that total, general auto accident, slip-and-fall, and personal injury advertisements accounted for approximately **$1 billion** [7]. Morgan & Morgan, the largest PI firm in the nation, alone spent an estimated **$218 million on advertising** in 2024, representing 8% of all legal services ads nationally [7].

Local television still captures approximately half of all law firm ad spending, with over $800 million going to linear TV [8]. However, connected TV (CTV) spending surged 241% between Q1 2023 and Q4 2025, signaling a rapid shift toward digital and streaming formats [8].

At the individual firm level, PI practices typically allocate 10-20% of revenue to marketing. Smaller firms are recommended to budget at least $3,000 to $5,000 per month for digital advertising across platforms like Google Ads, Facebook, and Local Service Ads [9].

## Key Trends

- **Declining traffic fatalities:** Motor vehicle fatalities dropped to an estimated 37,810 in 2025, down 12% from 42,789 in 2024, marking the 11th consecutive quarterly decline since Q2 2022 [10]. While fatal crashes are declining, non-fatal injury crashes remain consistently high, which is the more relevant pipeline for PI lead generation.
- **Rising settlement values:** Auto liability bodily injury claim averages increased 8% year-over-year in 2024 [2], driven by medical cost inflation and more aggressive plaintiff advocacy.
- **AI adoption:** 79% of legal professionals now incorporate AI tools into daily work, and firms with wide AI adoption are nearly 3x more likely to report revenue growth [2]. Common use cases include medical record summarization (56% interest), document analysis (48%), and automated brief drafting (34%) [4].
- **Digital-first client acquisition:** 75% of the US population uses online resources to research attorneys before making a decision [11], making digital presence and lead generation increasingly essential for firms that want to compete.
- **Aging population driving medical malpractice growth:** The 65+ population grew 3.1% between 2020 and 2025, increasing demand for healthcare services and the corresponding potential for medical malpractice claims [3].

# 3. Types of Personal Injury Cases

## Motor Vehicle Accidents (52% of all PI cases)

MVA is the cornerstone of personal injury law and the primary lead generation opportunity. This category includes:

- **Car accidents:** The most common PI case type overall. Average settlements range from $15,000 to $50,000, with a reported mean of approximately $37,000 [6]. Straightforward cases typically resolve in 6-9 months [4].
- **Truck accidents:** Cases involving commercial trucks (18-wheelers, delivery vehicles) carry significantly higher case values due to the severity of injuries and the involvement of commercial insurance policies with higher limits. Settlements often range from $50,000 to $500,000+.
- **Motorcycle accidents:** Higher injury severity leads to above-average settlements, with one firm reporting an average of approximately $66,000 [6].
- **Pedestrian and bicycle accidents:** Pedestrians and cyclists struck by vehicles sustain disproportionately severe injuries. Average settlements are reported around $67,000, reflecting the gravity of these incidents [6].
- **Rideshare accidents (Uber/Lyft):** A growing sub-category with complex insurance coverage questions involving the driver's personal policy, the rideshare company's commercial policy, and the at-fault party's coverage.
- **Hit-and-run accidents:** These cases involve uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage claims against the victim's own insurance, creating a different but still valuable case dynamic.

**Key volume stats:** Over 6,500 people are injured in car crashes daily in the US [2]. Approximately 2.6 million emergency department visits for motor vehicle crash injuries occurred in 2022 [12]. The total annual economic cost of motor vehicle crashes is approximately $417 billion [10].

## Slip and Fall / Premises Liability (approximately 22% of PI cases)

The second-largest PI category covers injuries sustained on another party's property due to hazardous conditions (wet floors, broken stairs, poor lighting, icy walkways). Settlements typically range from $10,000 to $150,000 depending on injury severity [4][6]. About 95% settle before trial [4]. Falls among adults 65 and older are a major driver, with 14 million older adults falling annually and over 41,000 dying from fall injuries in 2023 [2].

## Medical Malpractice (approximately 4-5% of formal PI filings)

Medical malpractice cases arise when healthcare providers deviate from the accepted standard of care, causing patient harm. These cases carry the highest average settlements in PI law. In 2024, there were over 10,000 medical malpractice payments totaling $4.33 billion, with an average payout of approximately $423,600 [6]. Jury trial awards average approximately $1.8 million [4]. However, these cases are notoriously difficult: success rates hover around 25-30%, and they typically take 2-3 years to resolve (4+ years if tried) [4]. Approximately 17,000 medical malpractice cases are filed annually [5].

## Product Liability

When defective or dangerous products cause injury, manufacturers, distributors, and retailers can be held liable. Jury trial awards average approximately $7 million, while pre-trial settlements range from $10,000 to $500,000 [4]. The plaintiff success rate is approximately 67% [4], and cases typically take 1-3 years to resolve.

## Workplace Injuries

While workers' compensation covers most on-the-job injuries, personal injury claims arise when a third party (not the employer) is responsible, or in cases of employer gross negligence. In 2023, there were 5,283 fatal work injuries and approximately 2.5 million nonfatal workplace injuries and illnesses [2]. The construction industry alone accounted for 1,008 fatal injuries, representing 21% of all work-related deaths [1]. Total workplace injury costs reached $176.5 billion in 2023 [2].

## Wrongful Death

When negligence or misconduct results in death, surviving family members can file wrongful death claims for loss of income, companionship, and funeral expenses. Settlements typically range from $500,000 to several million dollars depending on the deceased's age, income, and circumstances [6].

## Dog Bites and Animal Attacks

Dog bite claims averaged $58,545 per incident in 2024 [1]. These cases are governed by state-specific liability laws, with some states following strict liability (owner always responsible) and others requiring proof that the owner knew the dog was dangerous.

## Catastrophic Injuries (Cross-Category)

Regardless of how they occurred, catastrophic injuries command the highest settlements due to long-term care needs:

- **Traumatic brain injuries (TBI):** Approximately 2.8 million TBIs annually [1]. Settlements often exceed $100,000 and can reach millions for severe cases.
- **Spinal cord injuries:** Approximately 17,900 per year [1]. Settlements frequently exceed $500,000, and lifetime care costs range from $1.2 million to $5.1 million [1].

# 4. Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Contingency Fee** | The attorney's compensation model in PI cases. The lawyer receives a percentage of the recovery (typically 25-40%) only if the case is won. If the client recovers nothing, the attorney earns nothing. |
| **Statute of Limitations** | The legal deadline for filing a PI lawsuit. Varies by state, typically ranging from 1 to 6 years, with the majority of states setting a 2- or 3-year window. Twenty-six states use a 2-year deadline. Some states have separate, shorter windows for medical malpractice or claims against government entities. |
| **Comparative Negligence** | A legal doctrine that reduces the plaintiff's recovery by their percentage of fault. Comes in two forms: *pure* (plaintiff can recover even if 99% at fault, reduced proportionally) and *modified* (plaintiff is barred from recovery if their fault exceeds 50% or 51%, depending on the state). Approximately 10 states follow pure comparative negligence, 35 follow modified. |
| **Contributory Negligence** | A strict doctrine used in only 4 states (Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia) plus DC, where a plaintiff who is even 1% at fault is completely barred from recovery. |
| **Demand Letter** | A formal written document sent by the plaintiff's attorney to the insurance company outlining the injuries, treatment, liability, and the monetary amount being demanded. This letter initiates the negotiation process and is typically the first step toward settlement. |
| **Demand Package** | The complete package of evidence and documentation submitted with the demand letter, including medical records, bills, proof of lost wages, photographs, and a narrative of the claim. |
| **Settlement** | A negotiated agreement between the plaintiff and defendant (or their insurer) to resolve the case for a specified sum without going to trial. Approximately 95-96% of PI cases end in settlement. |
| **Verdict** | The decision rendered by a judge or jury after a trial. Verdicts in PI cases can be significantly higher than settlements but carry the risk of a defense verdict (plaintiff receives nothing). |
| **Personal Injury Protection (PIP)** | A type of auto insurance coverage required in no-fault states that covers the policyholder's own medical expenses and lost wages regardless of who caused the accident. Mandatory in 12 states. |
| **Medical Payments Coverage (MedPay)** | Similar to PIP but more limited in scope. Covers medical expenses for the policyholder and passengers after an accident, regardless of fault, but does not cover lost wages like PIP does. |
| **Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM)** | Insurance coverage that compensates the policyholder when the at-fault driver has no insurance (UM) or insufficient insurance (UIM) to cover the damages. Critical for hit-and-run cases. |
| **Case Value** | The estimated total monetary worth of a PI claim, determined by medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, future medical needs, and other damages. Case value heavily influences whether an attorney will accept the case. |
| **Retainer Agreement** | The contract between attorney and client that formalizes the legal representation, specifying the contingency fee percentage, expense responsibilities, and scope of services. |
| **Lien** | A legal claim against a settlement by a third party (typically a healthcare provider, health insurer, or government agency) for reimbursement of medical expenses they paid on the plaintiff's behalf. Liens reduce the plaintiff's net recovery. |
| **Subrogation** | The process by which an insurance company seeks reimbursement from the at-fault party's insurer for medical costs it paid on its policyholder's behalf. |
| **No-Fault State** | A state that requires drivers to carry PIP coverage and file claims with their own insurer first, regardless of fault. The injured party can only sue the at-fault driver if injuries meet a severity threshold (either a verbal or monetary threshold). Twelve states are no-fault: DE, FL, HI, KS, MA, MI, MN, NJ, NY, ND, OR, UT. Three of those (KY, NJ, PA) are "choice" no-fault states where drivers can opt out. |
| **Tort State (At-Fault State)** | A state where the at-fault driver's insurance is responsible for covering the injured party's damages. Thirty-eight states plus DC follow this model. These are the primary targets for PI lead generation since the legal claim process is more straightforward and attorney involvement is more common. |
| **Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI)** | The point at which a patient's condition has stabilized and further treatment is unlikely to produce significant improvement. Attorneys typically advise against settling before MMI, as settling too early may undervalue the claim. |
| **Pain and Suffering** | Non-economic damages compensating the plaintiff for physical pain, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and other intangible harms. Often calculated as a multiplier of economic damages (1.5x to 5x). |
| **Letter of Protection (LOP)** | A letter from the plaintiff's attorney to a healthcare provider guaranteeing payment from the settlement proceeds in exchange for the provider treating the plaintiff without immediate payment. |

# 5. The Lead Buyers

Personal injury leads are purchased by several distinct buyer segments, each with different needs, budgets, and quality expectations.

## Solo Practitioners and Small PI Firms (1-5 Attorneys)

This is the largest buyer segment by volume. Solo and small-firm PI attorneys handle the bulk of standard MVA and slip-and-fall cases. They typically lack in-house marketing teams and rely heavily on external lead sources to maintain their caseloads. These buyers are price-sensitive, typically spending $3,000 to $15,000 per month on lead acquisition, and they prioritize exclusive leads in their geographic service area. They value leads that come with verified contact information and a confirmed injury, since their capacity to work high volumes of unqualified leads is limited.

## Mid-Size PI Firms (6-25 Attorneys)

Mid-size firms have more capacity and often operate multi-office practices across a metro area or state. They can handle higher lead volumes and are willing to pay premium prices for exclusive, high-intent leads. Monthly marketing budgets range from $15,000 to $100,000+. Many mid-size firms also run their own PPC campaigns alongside purchased leads, and some have dedicated intake teams to process incoming leads quickly. They care most about lead-to-retained-case conversion rates and cost per signed case.

## National and Regional Mass Tort Firms

Large firms handling mass tort litigation (defective products, pharmaceutical injuries, environmental contamination) are aggressive lead buyers with massive budgets. These firms sometimes spend $25 million or more per month on marketing and lead acquisition [9]. They purchase leads at scale across the entire country and use sophisticated intake operations (often outsourced call centers) to screen and sign cases. For standard MVA leads, mass tort firms are less common buyers, though some diversified plaintiffs' firms handle both mass tort and individual PI.

## Legal Lead Aggregators and Referral Networks

A significant portion of PI leads flow through intermediary companies that aggregate leads from various sources and resell them to attorneys. Major aggregators include companies like Martindale-Nolo, Walker Advertising, 4LegalLeads, and On Point Legal Leads. Some aggregators sell shared leads (the same lead sold to 2-5 attorneys) at lower prices, while others offer exclusive leads at a premium. Attorney referral networks like Attorney Share also facilitate intra-attorney referrals, where a firm that receives a case outside its practice area or jurisdiction refers it to a qualified PI attorney for a referral fee.

## Intake and Case Acquisition Companies

A growing segment of the market consists of companies like Quintessa Marketing, Tort Experts, and ZeroRisk Cases that go beyond raw leads. They generate leads, qualify them through intake screening, and deliver signed retainer agreements to law firms at a fixed price per case. This model removes much of the conversion risk from the law firm, but at a significantly higher cost per case compared to raw leads.

# 6. Customer Demographics

The "customer" in PI lead generation is the accident victim seeking legal representation. Understanding who these people are, how they behave after an accident, and what drives them to seek an attorney is essential for targeting and messaging.

## Age

Motor vehicle accident injuries are most concentrated in the **19-44 age range**, with the highest crash rates among **teenagers and young adults** (crash rates for teens are nearly 4x higher than drivers aged 20+) [1]. However, PI claims span all adult ages. The 25-54 demographic represents the core of the lead pool for MVA cases, as these individuals are most likely to be driving regularly, employed (creating lost wages claims), and digitally active enough to respond to online advertising. Older adults (65+) dominate the slip-and-fall and medical malpractice categories, with 1 in 4 Americans over 65 experiencing a fall each year [2].

## Gender

Men account for 66.8% of fatal injury deaths, and the difference in injury rates between men and women is most extreme between the mid-20s and mid-60s [4]. However, **women are over 70% more likely than men to be injured (non-fatally) in motor vehicle crashes** [1], which means women may actually represent a larger share of the MVA lead pool since non-fatal injuries are what generate PI cases. Men account for 92% of workplace fatalities [1], making them the dominant demographic for work-related PI claims.

## Income and Socioeconomic Status

Lower-income individuals face disproportionately higher rates of accidental injury. Research has consistently shown that US counties with higher poverty rates experience more unintentional injuries [13]. Lower-income individuals are also more likely to walk or bike (increasing exposure to vehicle-pedestrian and vehicle-cyclist accidents), less likely to have newer vehicles with advanced safety features, and less likely to have adequate insurance coverage. These individuals often have the most to gain from legal representation, as insurance company lowball tactics are most effective against people who are financially desperate.

## Geography

Personal injury filings vary dramatically by state. **Florida leads the nation by a wide margin**, with 127.41 PI filings per 100,000 residents, followed by Ohio (30.38), New Jersey (30.34), South Carolina (30.12), and Illinois (22.95) [4]. Florida's pedestrian fatality rate is also the highest at 3.22 per 100,000 population [1]. Montana has the highest overall crash fatality rate at 22.6 per 100,000 [1]. Urban areas generate higher case volumes due to traffic density, while rural areas tend to see more severe accidents (higher speeds, longer emergency response times).

## Insurance Status

The uninsured motorist rate in the US hovers around 12-14%, meaning roughly 1 in 8 drivers lacks auto insurance. In some states (Mississippi, New Mexico, Florida), uninsured rates exceed 20%. Accident victims who are uninsured or underinsured face a different claims path and may have fewer options, though UM/UIM coverage on the at-fault party's side can still provide a viable claim.

## Who Searches for an Attorney

The injured person themselves is the primary searcher in the majority of cases, especially for moderate injuries where the victim is conscious and mobile. For severe injuries (hospitalization, TBI, spinal cord injuries), **family members** (spouses, parents, adult children) frequently conduct the attorney search on behalf of the injured person. Approximately **75% of Americans use online resources to research attorneys** [11], and internet searches are the primary method for selecting a lawyer among adults 18-24 and individuals earning over $150,000 per year [11]. For older demographics and lower-income individuals, television advertising, billboards, and word-of-mouth referrals remain influential.

## Search Behavior

Searches typically happen within 24-72 hours of the accident, with the highest intent occurring in the first 48 hours. Common search queries include "[city] car accident lawyer," "personal injury attorney near me," and "do I need a lawyer after a car accident." Mobile searches dominate, as many victims search from their phone while still at the scene, in the emergency room, or recovering at home.

# 7. Cost Landscape

## What the Consumer "Pays"

Personal injury cases operate on a contingency fee model, meaning the client pays no upfront costs. The attorney's compensation comes exclusively from the recovery:

- **Pre-litigation settlement (no lawsuit filed):** Typically **25-33%** contingency fee
- **Post-litigation settlement (lawsuit filed but settled before trial):** Typically **33-33.3%** contingency fee (the industry standard)
- **Trial verdict:** Typically **40%** contingency fee
- **Appeal:** Some agreements increase to **45%** if an appeal is necessary

Beyond the contingency fee, clients may also be responsible for **case expenses** (filing fees, expert witness fees, medical record retrieval costs, deposition costs), which are typically advanced by the attorney and deducted from the settlement. Medical liens from healthcare providers and health insurers further reduce the client's net recovery through subrogation claims.

# 8. Regulatory Landscape

## State Bar Attorney Advertising Rules

All 50 states have adopted some version of the ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct governing attorney advertising. Key restrictions include:

- **Truthfulness requirement:** All advertising must be truthful and not misleading. Claims about case results must include adequate disclosure of the circumstances, or a disclaimer stating that results depend on the facts of each case [15].
- **No false expertise claims:** Attorneys cannot call themselves "experts" or "specialists" unless they hold formal board certification in that area [15].
- **Anti-solicitation rules:** Many states prohibit direct solicitation (in-person, phone, or real-time electronic contact) of accident victims, particularly within **30 days of the incident** in some jurisdictions. Written communications (mail, email) are generally permitted but often must be clearly labeled as advertising [15].
- **Disclosure requirements:** Advertising must identify the attorney or firm responsible. Many states require specific disclaimers on advertisements, especially regarding past results and fee structures.
- **State-specific variation:** Rules differ significantly across states. Florida, Texas, New York, and California have some of the most detailed advertising regulations. Firms operating across state lines must comply with the rules of every state where they advertise [15].

## FTC Regulations

The Federal Trade Commission oversees truth-in-advertising standards that apply to lead generation operations:

- **Prohibition on deceptive practices:** Lead generators cannot use misleading domain names (e.g., domains that imply government affiliation), fabricate urgency, or misrepresent what the consumer is signing up for [16].
- **Consent documentation:** All consumer data collected for lead generation must be accompanied by documented consent showing the consumer knowingly submitted their information [16].
- **Supply chain liability:** The FTC holds all parties in the lead generation chain potentially liable, including lead generators, aggregators, and the law firms purchasing leads. Purchasers can be held responsible for deceptive advertising content used by their lead suppliers [16].
- **Retention requirements:** All creative materials, landing pages, and marketing materials should be retained for at least 3-5 years [16].

## TCPA Compliance (Telephone Consumer Protection Act)

The TCPA is the most critical regulatory framework for PI lead generation, particularly for operations that involve phone calls or text messages:

- **Prior express written consent:** Required before making any marketing calls or texts using an autodialer or prerecorded voice. Consent must be clear, conspicuous, and documented [17].
- **One-to-one consent rule (status):** The FCC's proposed rule requiring consent to be given to one specific seller at a time (rather than blanket consent to multiple parties) was originally set for January 2025 but was struck down by the Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals, which ruled the FCC lacked authority to redefine consent under the TCPA [17]. This rule is currently not in effect.
- **Consent revocation:** FCC updates effective April 2025 strengthened consumer rights to revoke consent through any reasonable method (text, email, voicemail, live chat, or even social media). Businesses must honor opt-outs promptly across all channels [17].
- **Penalties:** TCPA violations carry statutory damages of **$500 per violation** (per call or text), trebled to **$1,500 per willful violation** [17]. Class action TCPA lawsuits routinely result in multi-million dollar settlements.
- **Do Not Call (DNC) compliance:** Lead generators must scrub leads against the National Do Not Call Registry and honor internal DNC requests.

## No-Fault vs. Tort States

The distinction between no-fault and tort (at-fault) states fundamentally affects PI lead generation strategy:

**No-Fault States (12 states):** Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Dakota, Oregon, Utah. In these states, injured parties file claims with their own PIP insurer first and can only sue the at-fault driver if injuries meet a severity threshold (either a verbal threshold describing specific injury types or a monetary threshold requiring medical expenses to exceed a set dollar amount). This restricts the pool of cases that generate attorney involvement. Three states (Kentucky, New Jersey, Pennsylvania) offer "choice" no-fault, allowing drivers to opt between no-fault and tort coverage [18].

**Tort States (38 states + DC):** In at-fault states, the injured party files a claim against the at-fault driver's insurer and can pursue a lawsuit without meeting a severity threshold. These states represent the primary geographic targets for PI lead generation because attorney involvement is more common and claim processes are more conducive to legal representation [18].

## Comparative and Contributory Negligence

How states handle shared fault between parties significantly impacts case viability:

- **Pure Comparative Negligence (approximately 10 states, including CA, NY, and formerly FL):** Plaintiff can recover damages reduced by their percentage of fault, even if they are 99% responsible.
- **Modified Comparative Negligence (approximately 35 states):** Plaintiff is barred from recovery if their fault exceeds 50% (10 states follow this threshold) or 51% (24 states follow this threshold, including FL for most cases as of its recent reform).
- **Pure Contributory Negligence (4 states + DC: AL, MD, NC, VA):** Plaintiff is completely barred from recovery if they bear even 1% of fault. These are the most defense-friendly jurisdictions and the hardest markets for PI lead gen [19].

## Statute of Limitations Variation

Time limits for filing PI claims range from **1 year (Kentucky, Louisiana, Tennessee)** to **6 years (Maine, North Dakota)**, with the majority of states setting a **2-year (26 states)** or **3-year** deadline [20]. Twenty-two states have separate, shorter statutes for motor vehicle accidents or medical malpractice specifically. The discovery rule may extend the deadline in cases where the injury was not immediately apparent (common in medical malpractice and toxic exposure cases).
