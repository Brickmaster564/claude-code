# Personal Injury / MVA: Buyer Intelligence & Operational Playbook

**Date:** March 23, 2026
**Source:** Jakub (experienced PI lead gen operator) + Jasper's research

This document covers the buyer side of the PI lead gen business: how law firms think, what they pay, how to acquire them as clients, and how to message them. This is the operational foundation for selling PI leads.

---

## 1. The Economics (How Lawyers Make Money & What They'll Pay)

| Metric | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Retainer (signed client) cost | $1,500-$2,500 depending on state |
| Competitive states (e.g., CA) | ~$2,500 per retainer |
| Less competitive states | ~$1,500-$2,000 |
| Lawyer's cut of case value | ~33% (contingency fee) |
| Case values | $10K-$20K on the low end, up to $1M+ |
| Lead payouts | $200-$450 range (state-dependent) |

**Example math:** A $50K case nets the lawyer ~$15K in fees. Paying $2K for a signed retainer is very acceptable ROI. This is why lawyers are willing to pay $275-$450 per lead: the downstream economics justify it.

A "retainer" in legal context means a signed contract for representation, meaning the lawyer is now working the case. This is the metric that matters most to buyers.

---

## 2. What Buyers Actually Care About

Lawyers don't care about your CTR or CPM. They care about:

- **Cost per retainer signed** (e.g., $1,500 average)
- **Cost per case**
- **Lead quality / qualification criteria**
- **What other law firms you work with** (social proof)

Other metrics like conversion rates matter less if the bottom-line cost per signed case is good.

---

## 3. Lead Qualification: What Actually Matters for PI

OTP (one-time password verification) is less important than the actual qualifying factors:

- Accident details (when, where, how)
- Injury severity
- **Medical treatment received** (this is huge: treated injuries are far more valuable)
- At-fault screening
- Statute of limitations check
- Existing legal representation check
- Criminal history screening

Quiz framing matters: word questions to encourage truthful answers and make it harder for people to lie.

### Funnel Performance Benchmarks

- 12-17% conversion rate on quizzes without disqualifications
- Up to 30% depending on quiz length
- PI is harder to track due to many disqualifications in the quiz flow

## 4. Creative Strategies That Work for PI/MVA

### Visual Elements That Work

- Car crashes and worried people
- State driver's licenses (locally recognizable, stops the scroll)
- State maps and big settlement checks
- Comparisons of settlement amounts with vs. without a lawyer

### Messaging

- Money-related messaging works best: show dollar amounts, settlement sizes
- More aggressive does not equal non-compliant; reframe scripts to be truthful but bold
- Avoid "breaking news" style headlines (tested poorly)

### Ad Structure

- Create 4-5 hooks (first 3-5 seconds) + 3-4 ad bodies, then mix and match
- Aim for 12-25 different ad variations
- Try unusual voices: quiet/whispering voices, pattern interrupts (e.g., car crash sound in first 3 seconds)
- Reuse creatives with different voices to revive performance

### Campaign Setup (Example: California, 40 leads at $450 payout)

- Run 3 ad sets, 5-6 ads per set (same ads across sets)
- Budget at least $150/day
- Target broad or segment by age groups (25-35, 35-50, 50+)
- PI doesn't require specific demographic targeting: let Facebook's pixel learn

## 6. International Markets

### United States (Primary Market)

The biggest and most developed market for PI/MVA lead generation. Highly competitive, especially in states like California where CPLs run $100-$150 per lead. Law firms are accustomed to buying leads and have established intake processes.

### Australia

- Lower payouts than the US (MVA leads ~$100-$110 USD), but profit margins can hit 70% due to significantly lower competition and lead costs
- Major regulatory restrictions: Queensland prohibits direct response ads for lawyers (ads can only show "personal injury lawyer" + firm name). New South Wales and Victoria also have restrictions.
- Buyers exist but don't buy at US scale

### Canada

- PI exists, but limited direct experience from sources

### UK

- Medical negligence leads are cheap to generate but inconsistent in quality
- UK lawyers often want cases with claim values of £25,000+, which makes screening expensive and challenging
- Specific rules make it a harder market to enter

## 7. Buyer Acquisition: How to Get Law Firms

### Approach Priority

1. **Ads** (fastest and easiest, Jakub's top recommendation)
2. **Outreach** (works but inconsistent; it's a volume game, don't expect results from 10 firms/month)
3. **Recommended sequence:** LinkedIn > Email > Call

### Cost Per Booked Meeting (via ads)

- Historically $40-$50
- Now likely ~$100 due to increased costs
- Math: 10 calls x $100 = $1K spend, close 1 at $10K order = strong ROI, especially for recurring

### How Lawyers Currently Get Leads

- SEO
- Billboards
- Offline advertising
- Google PPC (expensive but common)
- Existing lead gen companies

### Outreach Tactics

- Manual, personalized outreach beats automation
- Short, straightforward messages (sometimes just one sentence)
- Make it clear it's not automated
- Create scarcity without sounding needy (e.g., "We're onboarding a new firm in your area")
- Use iMessage voice notes as follow-ups for higher engagement
- Use personal iPhone number to avoid spam flags
- Combine calls, SMS, and email

### Email Benchmarks

- 25-35% open rate on cold emails
- 10-20% reply rate on opens for natural-sounding sequences
- Replies often come after the second email
- Simple follow-ups like "checking on this" work well

## 8. Messaging Framework: How to Talk to PI Lawyers

Before crafting any message, internalize how PI lawyers think:

- They are bombarded with lead gen pitches constantly, and they've heard it all
- They are ego-driven and skeptical: they need to feel like you know what you're talking about
- They care about one thing above all else: **cost per signed retainer** (not CPL, not conversion rates, not your funnel tech)
- They already buy leads from somewhere (Google PPC, SEO, billboards, other lead gen companies): you're not introducing a new concept, you're asking them to switch or add a channel
- They respect confidence and preparation, not sales fluff

### Core Focal Points

#### 1. Lead with Economics, Not Features

Don't talk about your quiz, your funnel, your AI optimization. Talk about money.

**Wrong:** "We generate high-quality personal injury leads using advanced quiz funnels and AI-optimized Facebook campaigns"

**Right:** "Law firms we work with in [State] are signing retainers at an average cost of $1,500 per case"

Why this works: Lawyers do mental math instantly. If they know a case is worth $15K+ in fees and you're telling them they can acquire it for $1,500, the ROI is obvious. You don't need to explain anything else.

#### 2. State/Market Specificity

Generic outreach gets ignored. Hyper-local specificity signals that you actually operate in their market and aren't blasting 10,000 lawyers the same template.

**Right:** "We're onboarding a new personal injury firm in [City/State] this month"

**Right:** "In Georgia, firms we work with are buying MVA leads at $275 on average"

Why this works: It creates scarcity (limited spots in their area) and relevance (you understand their local market dynamics).

#### 3. Social Proof Without Oversharing

Lawyers want to know you work with other law firms, but they don't want to feel like they're getting recycled leads.

**Right:** "We currently work with [X] PI firms across [states]"

**Right:** "Happy to share performance benchmarks from similar markets"

Don't name specific firms unless you have permission. If asked, respond with: "I can't share firm names due to confidentiality, but I can walk you through the numbers from firms in comparable markets."

#### 4. Qualification Process as a Differentiator

Most lead gen companies send garbage, and lawyers know this. Highlighting your disqualification criteria immediately sets you apart:

**Right:** "We disqualify leads that don't meet medical treatment thresholds, at-fault accidents, and statute of limitations. You only pay for qualified leads."

List your actual qualification questions:
- When did the accident happen?
- Were you at fault?
- Have you sought medical treatment?
- Do you already have legal representation?
- Criminal history screening

Why this works: It shows you understand what makes a lead actually signable, not just a form fill.

### The Bottom Line

Don't sell leads. Sell the outcome. The outcome is **signed retainers at a predictable, profitable cost**. Everything in your messaging should point back to that.

## 9. Dealing with Lawyers: The Human Element

- Lawyers are often ego-driven, so confidence is everything
- Be extremely well-prepared for every possible question
- Role-play with your team before calls
- Sound confident, not salesy
- Have prepared answers for:
  - How you generate and qualify leads
  - Your conversion rates and costs
  - What other law firms you work with
- When pricing, give a range (e.g., "$200-$450") rather than a fixed price, and explain the variance by state/market
- Example: "In Georgia, law firms we work with buy leads for $275 on average, leading to $1.5K average cost per signed retainer"
