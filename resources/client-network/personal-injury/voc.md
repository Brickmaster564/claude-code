# Personal Injury / MVA -- Voice of Customer (VOC)

**Date:** March 23, 2026

---

## 1. Customer Avatar: The PI Lead

The lead is the **accident victim themselves**, typically within days to weeks of a motor vehicle accident. They are in pain, confused, financially stressed, and being pressured by insurance companies to settle quickly before they understand what their case is worth.

**Demographics:**
- Age 25-55 (peak driving years, highest injury rates)
- Slight male skew overall (66.8% of preventable injury deaths are male, per NSC), but women are well-represented in lead gen funnels
- Income $30K-$90K: high enough to have a car and job to lose, low enough that missed paychecks and $15,000+ in medical bills cause genuine financial crisis
- Concentrated in high-traffic, high-accident states: FL, CA, TX, GA, NY, OH, PA
- Many are uninsured or underinsured themselves (15.4% of all motorists are uninsured, and 1 in 3 drivers are uninsured or underinsured combined)
- Disproportionately hourly/gig workers who lose income immediately when they cannot work

**Life Situation Triggers:**
- Rear-ended at a stoplight, T-boned at an intersection, hit by a distracted or drunk driver
- Went to the ER, got discharged with pain meds, told to "follow up with your doctor"
- Woke up the next morning and could barely move (delayed onset of whiplash, soft tissue injuries)
- Insurance adjuster called within 24-48 hours with a lowball offer and a release form
- Car is totaled and they have no way to get to work
- Medical bills started arriving: $3,300 average ER visit, ambulance ride alone can be $2,000+
- Missed one, two, three weeks of work and the paychecks stopped
- Started Googling "do I need a lawyer after a car accident" from their phone

**Psychographic Profile:**
- In physical pain and emotionally rattled, often simultaneously dealing with both
- Feels powerless against an insurance company that has adjusters, lawyers, and scripts designed to minimize payouts
- Confused about their rights, what they are entitled to, and what the process even looks like
- Distrustful of lawyers ("ambulance chasers") but increasingly desperate for help
- Angry at the other driver, angry at the insurance company, and angry at a system that seems designed to screw them over
- Afraid the pain will never go away, afraid the bills will never stop, and afraid no one will believe how much they are suffering
- Many are dealing with invisible injuries (whiplash, soft tissue damage, PTSD) that do not show up on imaging but are devastatingly real

## 6. Objections (Why They Haven't Hired a Lawyer Yet)

### "I can't afford a lawyer"
- The most common objection, and the most easily overcome. Accident victims assume hiring a lawyer requires upfront payment, like a retainer or hourly fee.
- **Reality:** 99% of personal injury lawyers work on contingency: they only get paid if you win. The standard fee is 33% of the settlement (25% for quick settlements, up to 40% if the case goes to trial). Most also offer free initial consultations to evaluate your case. You pay nothing upfront, nothing out of pocket, and nothing at all if the case does not succeed.

### "My case isn't serious enough"
- Many people believe that only catastrophic injuries (paralysis, traumatic brain injury, death) qualify for a personal injury claim. If they can still walk and talk, they assume their case has no value.
- **Reality:** Whiplash, soft tissue injuries, herniated discs, concussions, and chronic pain are all compensable injuries, even when they do not show up on an MRI. The average car accident injury settlement is $37,200, and most of those cases involve non-catastrophic injuries. If you have medical bills and missed work, you likely have a case.

### "The insurance company already made me an offer"
- The adjuster called quickly, was friendly, and offered a number that seemed reasonable in the moment, especially when bills were piling up and the victim was desperate for cash.
- **Reality:** Insurance adjusters are trained to settle fast, before victims know the full extent of their injuries. The average first offer is around $11,000, while the average subsequent settlement is $42,500. People with lawyers receive an average of $77,600 compared to $17,600 without representation. That initial offer is almost never what the case is actually worth.

### "I don't want to sue anyone"
- Many people hear "lawyer" and think "lawsuit," which triggers anxiety about confrontation, courtrooms, and dragging everything out. They just want to move on.
- **Reality:** Over 95% of personal injury cases settle out of court without ever going to trial (Bureau of Justice Statistics). Hiring a lawyer typically means they negotiate with the insurance company on your behalf. Most cases resolve in 6-14 months. You almost certainly will never see the inside of a courtroom.

### "Lawyers are all scammers / ambulance chasers"
- The personal injury industry has a reputation problem. "Ambulance chaser" stereotypes, aggressive TV ads, and stories of case runners create deep distrust.
- **Reality:** Ambulance chasing is illegal in 21 states, and legitimate lawyers are bound by strict ethical codes. The contingency fee model means the lawyer's financial interest is aligned with yours: they only make money if they get you more money. Read reviews, ask for referrals, schedule consultations with multiple firms, and choose someone who makes you feel heard rather than pressured.

### "It will take years"
- Victims imagine a multi-year process with depositions, courtrooms, and endless paperwork. They cannot afford to wait that long, emotionally or financially.
- **Reality:** About 44% of personal injury cases settle within one year of filing. The median resolution time is 13-14 months. Straightforward MVA cases often settle in 6-9 months after medical treatment concludes. Your lawyer can also help you access medical treatment on a lien basis, meaning you get treated now and pay from the settlement later.

### "I don't have time for this"
- They are already dealing with doctor's appointments, physical therapy, work disruptions, and daily life. Adding a legal process feels like one more thing they cannot handle.
- **Reality:** A good personal injury lawyer handles the entire process: gathering records, negotiating with insurance, managing paperwork, and keeping the case moving. The client's main job is attending medical appointments and recovering. Multiple testimonials confirm: "They took care of everything. I could focus on healing."

### "I was partially at fault"
- They were going a few miles over the speed limit, or changed lanes right before impact, or were looking at their phone for a second. They assume any fault on their part disqualifies them entirely.
- **Reality:** The vast majority of states use comparative negligence, meaning you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault, just reduced by your percentage of fault. In a 50% bar state, you can recover as long as you are less than 50% at fault. Even 30% fault leaves 70% of the damages on the table. Only a handful of states use the harsher contributory negligence standard.

## 8. Emotional Journey Map

### Stage 1: Shock / "I'm Fine"
**Timeline:** Immediately after the accident through the first 24-72 hours

"I thought I was okay. The adrenaline was pumping and I walked away from the car. I told the paramedics I was fine."

- Adrenaline masks the pain. The victim is focused on the immediate crisis: exchanging information, calling insurance, getting the car towed, maybe going to the ER "just in case."
- They minimize their injuries to others and to themselves. "It could have been worse." "At least no one died."
- They are shaken but functional. They might post about it on social media, call family, try to go to work the next day.
- **Emotional state:** adrenaline, disbelief, relief at being alive, mild anxiety, denial of severity.

### Stage 2: Overwhelm / "This Is Getting Worse"
**Timeline:** Days 3-14 after the accident

"I woke up the next morning and could barely move. The pain was so much worse than the day of the accident. Then the insurance company called with an offer that wouldn't even cover my ER bill."

- Delayed symptoms emerge: stiffness, headaches, radiating pain, trouble sleeping, anxiety while driving. Whiplash and soft tissue injuries often peak 48-72 hours after impact.
- The insurance adjuster calls, friendly but persistent, pushing for a recorded statement and a quick settlement. The number they offer feels insultingly low.
- Medical bills start arriving. The ER bill, the ambulance, the follow-up appointment. Each envelope is a punch to the gut.
- They start Googling: "do I need a lawyer after a car accident," "what is my case worth," "insurance lowball offer what do I do."
- **Emotional state:** escalating pain, confusion, frustration, growing anger at the insurance company, first waves of anxiety about money.

### Stage 3: Desperation / "I Need Help"
**Timeline:** Weeks 2-8 after the accident

"The bills kept coming. I couldn't work. The insurance company stopped returning my calls. I realized I was completely on my own against a billion-dollar company, and I was losing."

- Financial pressure mounts: missed paychecks, bills going to collections, credit cards maxed out to cover basic expenses. The gap between what insurance offered and what the accident actually cost becomes painfully clear.
- Physical recovery stalls or symptoms worsen. Multiple doctor visits, physical therapy, maybe an MRI that shows "nothing" even though the pain is very real.
- Emotional toll compounds: PTSD symptoms appear (nightmares, avoidance, hypervigilance), anxiety about driving, depression, irritability affecting relationships.
- They realize they cannot handle this alone. The legal system, the medical system, and the insurance system are all working against them. This is the moment they pick up the phone.
- **Emotional state:** desperation, anger, helplessness, fear of financial ruin, decision point: "I need a lawyer."

### Stage 4: Relief / "I Wish I'd Done This Sooner"
**Timeline:** After hiring an attorney

"From the beginning, I felt heard and understood. Sean truly took a weight off my shoulders the minute we started working together."

- The lawyer takes over communication with the insurance company. No more ignored calls, no more lowball pressure, no more recorded statement traps.
- Medical treatment continues, often on a lien basis so the victim does not have to pay out of pocket during the case.
- The process moves forward without the victim carrying the administrative burden. They can focus on healing.
- The settlement comes in far higher than the insurance company's initial offer. Bills are paid. Lost wages are recovered. There is money left over.
- **Emotional state:** relief, cautious optimism, gratitude, empowerment, "I wish I had called sooner."

## 9. Channel-Specific Language Patterns

### Facebook / Instagram (Interruption: awareness and early consideration)
- Lead with the insurance company as the villain: "The insurance company's first offer is almost NEVER what your case is worth."

- Use the financial fear: "If a car accident left you buried in medical bills and missing paychecks, you might be owed more than you think."
- Address the objection directly: "Think you can't afford a lawyer? Most personal injury attorneys charge $0 upfront."
- Visual: real-looking (not stock) imagery of car damage, someone holding their neck, medical bills spread on a table, someone on the phone looking stressed

- Key hook formats: "Were you in a car accident in [State/City]?", "The insurance company doesn't want you to know this", "3 mistakes to avoid after a car accident"

- Match exact-intent queries: "car accident lawyer near me," "do I need a lawyer after car accident," "how much is my car accident case worth," "personal injury lawyer free consultation"
- Lead with value + urgency: "Injured in a Car Accident? Free Case Review. No Fee Unless You Win."

- Include specificity: mention the state/city, mention MVA specifically, include "free consultation" in every ad

### YouTube Pre-Roll (Interruption: awareness)
- Open with the emotional hook in the first 3 seconds: "If you were hurt in a car accident and the insurance company is pressuring you to settle, stop."

### Reddit / Forums / Organic Search (Information-seeking: pre-consideration)
- These users are Googling from their phones in the days after an accident. They are reading r/legaladvice, r/personalinjury, r/insurance, and Avvo Q&A threads.
- Their language is raw and unfiltered: "I was rear-ended and don't know what to do," "the insurance company offered me $500, is that normal?," "should I get a lawyer for a minor car accident?"
- Content that captures this traffic should be educational, non-salesy, and answer the exact question they typed: "What to do if the insurance company lowballs you," "How much is a car accident settlement worth?," "Can I still get compensation if I was partially at fault?"

- "I don't want to get scammed by some ambulance chaser"

### What they FEEL (emotional level)

- Fear that the pain will not go away, that the bills will keep coming, and that they will lose everything they have worked for
- Helplessness against a system designed by and for insurance companies, not for injured people
- Shame about needing help, about not being able to work, about being a "burden" on their family
- Exhaustion from the constant pain, the sleepless nights, the financial stress, and the emotional toll of reliving the accident over and over

### What they truly DESIRE (identity level)
- To feel like they matter, that their pain and suffering are real and valid, and that someone powerful is fighting for them
- To reclaim the life they had before the accident: their health, their income, their independence, their sense of safety
- To feel protected and taken care of during the most vulnerable period of their life, instead of being exploited by the people who are supposed to help them
- To have justice: not revenge, but fairness, where the person who caused this is held accountable and they are made whole
- To look back and feel proud that they stood up for themselves instead of accepting less than they deserved
