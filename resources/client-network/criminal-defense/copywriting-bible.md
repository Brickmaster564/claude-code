### Affordability / Cost Reframe

1. Think you can't afford a DUI lawyer? A conviction will cost you $10,000 to $25,000 over the next few years. The lawyer is the cheapest part.
2. A DUI attorney costs less than one year of your insurance increase after a conviction.

4. Your insurance goes up $1,500 to $2,500 a year for 3 to 7 years after a DUI conviction. A lawyer who gets the charge reduced pays for themselves many times over.

### Skepticism / "Is It Worth It?"

1. "I blew a .14. What's the point of fighting it?" 25% of DUI cases are dismissed nationally. The breathalyzer is not the end of your case.
2. Your public defender has 11 other DUI cases today. And they won't be at your DMV hearing. Think about what that means for your license.
3. An Ohio man blew a .163. His attorney got the charges dismissed. The facts are only the starting point.
4. "My friend said a lawyer can't do anything for a DUI." Your friend was wrong. More than half of contested DUI cases in Texas result in reduction or dismissal.

### Urgency / The Clock

1. You have 10 days from your arrest to request a DMV hearing. Miss it and you automatically lose your license.
2. The first 48 hours after a DUI arrest are the most important for your case. Evidence degrades. Deadlines expire. Waiting costs you options.
3. Every day you wait is a day closer to your court date with no defense and no plan. Call before the deadline passes.

### Social Proof / Normalization

1. Over 800,000 Americans are arrested for DUI every year. One every 39 seconds. If it happened to you, you are far from alone.
2. "I know it feels like the end of the world right now. At least that's what it felt like for me." It gets better. But only if you take the right steps.
3. "I was absolutely terrified after I was arrested, but once I spoke to my attorney, he put me at ease." That call changes everything.
4. Most DUI cases are filed against people with no prior record who made one mistake on one night. A good attorney knows the difference between a mistake and a conviction.

### Specificity / Stats

1. 1 in 4 DUI cases gets dismissed. Don't assume yours can't be.
2. Breathalyzers need regular calibration. Field sobriety tests can be challenged. The stop itself might not hold up. There are more options than you think.
3. 90% of DUI cases end in a plea deal, not a trial. What matters is the deal your attorney negotiates.
4. A DUI conviction can block you from entering Canada for life. One border agent. One database check. One denial.

## 11. Under-the-Fingernail Copy Blocks

### Block 1: The Morning After

It's 7 AM on a Sunday. You haven't slept. Your hands are shaking. You're sitting in your apartment, still wearing the clothes from last night, and the only thing running through your mind is an endless loop of what happened and what's going to happen next.

You keep picking up your phone, then putting it down. You Google "first DUI penalties" and the first result says you could face up to a year in jail. Your stomach drops. You close the tab. You open it again. You search "will I lose my job for a DUI" and the results are worse.

You think about calling your parents. You can't. You think about telling your girlfriend. You don't know how. You think about calling a lawyer, but the idea of saying the words out loud to a stranger makes your throat close.

So you sit there. Scrolling. Reading horror stories. Calculating costs you can't afford. Wondering if your life just changed forever because of one decision on one night that felt completely normal at the time.

Here's what you need to know right now: this feeling is temporary. The panic is real, but the situation is manageable. 25% of DUI cases are dismissed. The total cost depends entirely on the next decision you make. And the only thing standing between "worst night of your life" and "a mistake you handled the right way" is one phone call to someone who has been through this with hundreds of people just like you.

### Block 2: The Hidden Cost

You think the DUI costs what the judge says it costs. The fine. Maybe some classes. Maybe a suspended license for a few months. You can handle that. You'll just plead guilty, pay the fine, and move on.

Then the insurance bill arrives. Your premium went from $120 a month to $400. That's $3,360 more per year. For the next 5 years, that's $16,800 in insurance increases alone, on top of everything else.

Then you find out about the ignition interlock device. $150 to install, $100 a month to maintain. Every time you start your car, you blow into a tube in front of whoever is sitting next to you. Your coworker. Your date. Your kid. For 12 to 18 months.

Then the background check. You apply for a promotion. The HR system flags a criminal conviction. The interview you prepared for never happens. The job you wanted goes to someone with a clean record.

Then the border. You book a trip to Canada. The border agent runs your passport. A DUI conviction is grounds for denial of entry. You get turned around.

The fine is the smallest part of a DUI. The conviction is what costs you. And the difference between a conviction and a dismissal, between a criminal record and a clean one, often comes down to whether you had the right attorney in the first 10 days.

### Block 3: The Identity Crisis

Before last night, you knew exactly who you were. A good person. A responsible adult. Someone who goes to work, pays their bills, and does the right thing. You've never had a criminal record. You've never been in the back of a police car. You've never had someone put handcuffs on your wrists.

And now you're Googling "do I need a lawyer for a DUI" at 3 AM with a court date you don't understand and a system you've never been inside before. The word "criminal" keeps appearing on your screen and it doesn't match anything you've ever believed about yourself.

The shame is the worst part. Worse than the fines. Worse than the license. Worse than the fear of jail. Because the shame attacks who you are, not just what happened. You keep thinking: "How could I have been so stupid? How is this my life?"

Here's what 800,000 other people a year already know: a DUI doesn't make you a criminal. It makes you someone who made one mistake on one night. And the gap between that mistake defining your future and you moving past it comes down to the next 10 days.

A DUI attorney has sat across from someone in your exact situation hundreds of times. They've heard your BAC number. They've seen your charge sheet. They've handled your fear. And they've turned it into a plan. That's all this is right now: the moment before you have a plan. Once you have one, the panic starts to lift.

The consultation is free. The call is confidential. And the person on the other end is not there to judge you. They're there to tell you what happens next.
