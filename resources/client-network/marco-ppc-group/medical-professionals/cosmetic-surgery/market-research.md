# Cosmetic Surgery: Market Research

**Date:** March 24, 2026
**Context:** B2B outreach targeting cosmetic surgery practices as potential PPC clients for Marco (PPCSauce). Not a PPL vertical. Research purpose: understand the ICP, find them, and write outreach that lands.

---

## 1. Market Overview

- The US cosmetic surgery market is valued at approximately **$21.6 billion in 2025**, projected to reach $41.3 billion by 2034 at a 7.4% CAGR (Precedence Research, 2025)
- Approximately **7,000 board-certified plastic surgeons** hold ABPS (American Board of Plastic Surgery) certification in the US, with ASPS representing 92% of them (over 11,000 members globally including Canada)
- An estimated **10,400+ cosmetic surgery clinics** operate across the US (ClickPlacement industry analysis, 2025), and the market is highly fragmented with no single company holding more than 5% market share (IBISWorld)
- **6.1 million+ cosmetic procedures** were performed in 2024 (ASPS 2024 Statistics Report), with surgical procedures up 1% and minimally invasive procedures up 3% year over year
- The surgical procedure segment accounts for **80% of market revenue**, while minimally invasive treatments (Botox, fillers) drive volume: 9.88 million neuromodulator treatments and 5.33 million hyaluronic acid filler sessions in 2024
- Top surgical procedures by volume: liposuction, breast augmentation, tummy tucks, breast lifts, and eyelid surgery (ASPS, 2024)
- The female segment accounts for **84% of market share** by gender (Precedence Research, 2024)
- Hospitals and specialty clinics captured **59% of revenue** in 2024, with the remainder split across ambulatory surgical centers and office-based surgical facilities
- The **GLP-1 effect** is emerging as a growth driver: over 837,000 patients prescribed GLP-1 medications by ASPS member surgeons in 2024 went on to pursue further aesthetic care (body contouring after weight loss)
- Private equity transaction volume in plastic surgery surged **440%** from 5 deals in 2022 to 27 in 2024 (FOCUS Investment Banking), signaling consolidation pressure on independent practices

### Patient Lifetime Value

Patient lifetime value (LPV) in cosmetic surgery is significantly higher than a single procedure because patients who undergo one procedure tend to invest in further enhancements over time. The cross-sell path runs from surgical procedures into injectables (Botox, fillers), skincare treatments, and med spa services.

- **Botox-only patient LPV example:** $600 every 6 months for 5 years = $6,000 revenue, $2,000 profit (MedEsthetics)
- **Surgical patient who converts to maintenance:** one $8,000 procedure + $1,200/year in injectables over 5 years = $14,000+ LPV
- **High-value surgical patient:** multiple procedures (breast aug + rhinoplasty + ongoing Botox) = $25,000-$50,000+ over the relationship
- Injectable margins run 70-80%, making recurring non-surgical patients extremely profitable per visit (AestheticsPro, 2025)
- Loyalty programs can increase revenue by 15-25% when properly implemented (McKinsey)

If a practice spends $5,000/month on Google Ads and converts just 2-3 consultations into procedures averaging $8,000-$12,000, that represents $16,000-$36,000 in revenue from a $5,000 investment. Successful practices report 5-10x ROAS when campaigns are properly optimized. The economics work overwhelmingly in favor of PPC when it is run competently, which is exactly why surgeons are so frustrated when agencies waste their budgets on broad match keywords and vanity metrics.

## 3. ICP Profile

### Subgroup A: Solo and Small Cosmetic Surgery Practices (1-3 surgeons)

**Who they are:**
- Board-certified plastic surgeons (ABPS) or cosmetic surgeons operating private practices, typically 3-15 total employees including front office staff, patient coordinators, nurses, and medical aestheticians
- Revenue ranges from $1M-$3M/year for solo practitioners, $3M-$7M for small multi-surgeon practices
- Primarily cosmetic (elective, cash-pay) rather than reconstructive (insurance-based), though many do both
- The surgeon is simultaneously the practitioner, business owner, and final decision-maker on all marketing spend

**What they sell:**
- Core surgical procedures: breast augmentation, rhinoplasty, facelift, liposuction, tummy tuck, blepharoplasty, body contouring
- Non-surgical aesthetics: Botox, dermal fillers, laser treatments, chemical peels
- Many operate an integrated med spa generating recurring revenue from injectables and skincare
- Consultations (often $100-$250, applied to procedure cost if booked)

**Decision-maker profile:**
- The surgeon-owner in virtually all cases, sometimes with input from a practice manager or patient coordinator who handles day-to-day operations
- Marketing sophistication varies widely: some have tried multiple agencies and platforms and are deeply skeptical, while others have relied almost entirely on word-of-mouth and Instagram and are exploring paid advertising for the first time
- Time-poor by definition: running consultations, performing surgeries, managing post-ops, and overseeing staff. They have zero patience for agencies that require hand-holding or produce reports they have to decode
- They measure everything in booked procedures and revenue, not impressions, clicks, or followers
- Typical age range: 40-60. They completed lengthy surgical training and often started practices after years in hospital or group settings

**How they find vendors:**
- Peer referrals from other surgeons (professional societies, conferences, informal networks)
- Direct outreach from agencies (they receive a lot of it, most of it generic)
- Google search when actively seeking help ("PPC for plastic surgeons," "cosmetic surgery marketing agency")
- Industry conferences (ASPS meetings, The Aesthetic Meeting, medical marketing conferences)
- Social proof matters enormously: they want to see case studies from other practices their size

### Subgroup B: Multi-Location and Group Practices (3+ surgeons)

**Who they are:**
- Mid-size practices with multiple surgeons, often across 2-5 locations
- Revenue $5M-$20M+, with dedicated marketing coordinators or practice administrators
- More likely to have experience with agencies, in-house marketing staff, or both
- Increasingly targets of private equity rollups (440% deal growth 2022-2024)

**Why they're distinct as PPC prospects:**
- They have bigger budgets ($10,000-$50,000+/month on Google Ads) but also higher expectations
- The decision-maker is often a practice administrator or marketing director rather than the surgeon directly, though the surgeon retains veto power
- They are more likely to evaluate agencies on data and infrastructure (tracking, CRM integration, reporting dashboards) rather than just "did the phone ring"
- The pitch needs to emphasize scale, multi-location targeting, and sophisticated attribution

## 4. Competitive Landscape (Agencies/Vendors They've Dealt With)

### Major Vendors and Their Reputations

| Vendor | Primary Complaint |
|---|---|
| **RealSelf** | Pay-to-play directory disguised as a review site. Doctors pay $200-$5,000/month depending on market (NYC/LA at the top). Complaints include "heavy monthly fees with no leads," calls from patients 400+ miles away, free trials that convert into non-refundable 3-month contracts, and deleted negative reviews. One doctor called it "a racket and a waste of time." ROI data shows ~1 visitor/day to practice websites, with Google Ads generating 2-3x more conversions for equivalent spend (MyAdvice case study). |
| **Zocdoc** | Charges $70-$115 per appointment booked regardless of whether patients show up. Practices report overwhelming cancellation rates (3-5x/week), fake/phantom patients blocking real slots, and a "predatory one-sided business model." Rated 1.6 stars from complaints on ComplaintsBoard. |
| **Scorpion** | Aggressive in legal marketing, expanding into medical. Contract lock-in with proprietary systems: if you leave, you lose your website, domain, content, and ad account access. Clients report declining service quality after the initial sale, vanity metric reporting, and poor targeting (ranking for "free consult" and "cheap" keywords). |
| **PatientPop / Tebra** | Template websites and SEO packages that blend all practices together. Complaints center on generic strategies, slow results, and difficulty extracting data when leaving. |
| **Generic agencies (WebFX, etc.)** | Not healthcare-specific. Surgeons report having to explain the difference between a tummy tuck consult and a Botox inquiry to account managers who use the same playbook for every client. Cookie-cutter campaigns that waste budget on irrelevant keywords. |
| **Incredible Marketing** | Healthcare-specific but complaints about account management quality declining over time and difficulty getting responsive support once the contract is signed. |
| **Clutch/directories** | "Only fake/spammy leads... after 8 months, spent over $10,000 for no leads and bad customer service." (Trustpilot review) Year-long commitments with no results. |

### The Trust Dynamic

Cosmetic surgeons are high-income professionals who run businesses generating $1M-$10M+ in annual revenue, yet most have been burned at least once by a marketing vendor. The frustration pattern breaks down into four categories.

First, agencies that treat cosmetic surgery like any other vertical. Surgeons are tired of explaining their business to people who should already understand it. A tummy tuck consult is not the same as a Botox inquiry, and a rhinoplasty keyword campaign has completely different economics from a breast augmentation campaign. When account managers don't know the difference, the surgeon's confidence evaporates immediately.

Second, agencies that hide behind vanity metrics. The surgeon doesn't care about impressions, followers, or click-through rates. They care about one thing: how many new patients booked procedures this month, and what did it cost to acquire them. Agencies that report on "trending in the right direction" while the consultation calendar stays half-empty get fired.

Third, agencies that hold assets hostage. Websites built on proprietary platforms, Google Ads accounts owned by the agency, domains registered under agency credentials. When the surgeon tries to leave, they discover their entire digital presence belongs to someone else. This is the rage point that drives the most visceral anger.

Fourth, the chain clinic arms race. Independent surgeons are competing against Sono Bello, Ideal Image, and other cosmetic surgery chains that spend millions on advertising. These chains outbid independents on Google Ads, dominate local search results, and use aggressive pricing to commoditize procedures. Independent surgeons feel squeezed between rising ad costs and chain-clinic competition, which makes every wasted marketing dollar feel twice as painful.

PPCSauce's positioning directly counters all four: specialist PPC knowledge eliminates the education gap, performance-based accountability replaces vanity metrics, full account ownership removes the hostage dynamic, and expert campaign management lets independents compete efficiently against chain budgets.

## 6. Top Target Metros

Ranked by surgeon density, procedure volume, Google Ads competition, and ad spend concentration:

1. **Miami, FL** - Cosmetic surgery capital of the US. Highest surgeon density per capita (3.90 per 100,000 residents). Leads the nation in liposuction and BBL volume. CPCs are premium but procedure values match. Extremely competitive, which means practices are spending heavily on Google Ads and feeling the pain of rising costs.

2. **Los Angeles, CA** - Second-highest surgeon density (2.98 per 100k). Dominates rhinoplasty search volume. Home to some of the highest procedure fees in the country. Practices compete against celebrity surgeons and chain clinics simultaneously, creating intense PPC pressure.

3. **New York City, NY** - 249 total plastic surgeons (most by raw count). Despite ranking 42nd per capita due to population size, the absolute volume of Google Ads competition is enormous. CPCs in Manhattan for cosmetic surgery terms are among the highest in the country.

4. **Dallas-Fort Worth, TX** - Rapid population growth driving new practice formation. Strong cosmetic surgery demand with lower cost of living than coastal markets, meaning PPC budgets stretch further for practices but competition is intensifying fast.

5. **Houston, TX** - Large, diverse metro with significant cosmetic surgery demand, particularly for body contouring and rhinoplasty. Growing number of practices competing on Google for the same keywords.

6. **San Francisco / San Jose, CA** - High surgeon density (San Jose 4th, SF 5th per capita nationally). Tech-affluent patient base drives premium pricing and willingness to invest in PPC. High CPCs but strong ROI potential.

7. **Atlanta, GA** - Southeast hub for cosmetic surgery. Growing demand, increasing competition, and a large population of practices spending on Google Ads. BBL and body contouring are particularly popular.

8. **Chicago, IL** - Major metro with dense surgeon concentration. Competitive CPCs but more affordable than coastal markets. Year-round demand with seasonal spikes.

9. **Scottsdale / Phoenix, AZ** - One of the fastest-growing cosmetic surgery markets. Rapid influx of California transplants driving demand. Many practices are digitally native and already investing in PPC.

10. **Denver, CO** - Strong demand, growing population, and tech-savvy practice owners who understand digital advertising. Competitive but not yet as saturated as the top 5 metros.

**Secondary targets with strong potential:** Salt Lake City (2nd highest surgeon density per capita), San Diego, Nashville, Austin, Tampa, Charlotte, and Washington D.C. (leads in BBL search interest per capita). These markets have lower CPCs, less agency fatigue among practice owners, and untapped PPC opportunity.
