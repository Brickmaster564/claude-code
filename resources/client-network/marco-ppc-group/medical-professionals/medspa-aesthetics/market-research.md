# Medical Professionals: Med Spa & Aesthetics: Market Research

**Date:** March 24, 2026
**Context:** B2B outreach targeting med-spas and aesthetic medicine practices as potential PPC clients for Marco (PPCSauce). Not a PPL vertical. Research purpose: understand the ICP, find them, and write outreach that lands.

---

## 1. Market Overview

- **$17+ billion** US medical aesthetics industry as of 2024, growing by more than $1 billion per year (AmSpa 2024 State of the Industry Report)
- **10,488** med spa locations in the US as of 2023, up from 8,899 in 2022 and just 1,600 in 2010 (AmSpa)
- Projected to reach **12,000 locations by 2027**
- US med spa market valued at **$7.4-8.4 billion** in 2025, growing at **14-15% CAGR** through 2034-2035 (Grand View Research, Precedence Research)
- Global med spa market projected to hit **$87.9 billion by 2034** (Precedence Research)
- **90% independently owned**, only 10% platform or investment-backed (Apex Leaders)
- **81% operate as single locations** (AmSpa 2024)
- Multi-location operators now average **9 locations per group**, up from 6 in 2022, indicating slow consolidation
- **~70% of med spa owners are women**, ~90% of patients are women (AmSpa)
- Top 4 states by concentration: California (11.5%), New York (10.0%), Florida (8.8%), Texas (7.6%)

This is a massively fragmented, fast-growing market dominated by small independent operators who need marketing but lack the internal expertise to run it well. The 90/10 independent-to-platform split means the vast majority of potential PPC clients are owner-operators making their own vendor decisions.

|---|---|---|

| Weight loss (semaglutide) | $18,000 | $30,000 |
| Hormone therapy | $12,500 | $20,000 |

(Source: Dean Garland LTV analysis, 2025)

- **65% of patients are repeat patients** (AmSpa). Repeat clients spend 67% more than new ones.
- **47% average repeat visit rate** across US med spas (Workee 2025 stats)
- Practices using automated follow-ups achieve **61-67% retention**
- Average client stays **3 years**, visits **3-4 times per year**
- CLV formula: (average visits per year x average spend per visit) x years retained
- Example: 4 visits x $650/visit x 3 years = **$7,800 CLV before product sales**
- "LTV is a design choice, not a data point. Your pricing, packaging, and follow-up systems determine whether a patient's five-year value is $900 or $12,000." (Dean Garland)

## 3. ICP Profile

### Who They Are

- **Solo practitioners or small group practices** (1-10 staff), representing the vast majority of the 10,000+ US med spas
- Owner-operators who are also the clinical lead: an MD, DO, NP, PA, or RN who built a practice around injectable and laser services
- Predominantly female owners (~70%) running practices that serve ~90% female clientele
- Located in affluent suburbs and urban metros where discretionary income supports elective cosmetic spending
- Annual revenue $500K-3M, with $1.4-2M as the sweet spot

### Decision Makers

| Title | Description |
|---|---|
| Owner/Founder | The physician, NP, or PA who started the practice. Controls budget and strategy. |
| Medical Director | The MD/DO overseeing clinical operations. Sometimes the owner, sometimes contracted. |
| Practice Manager/Operations Manager | Handles day-to-day operations including vendor relationships and marketing budget in larger practices. |
| Nurse Practitioner (NP) | In states that allow NP ownership (CO, CT, NM, OR, AZ, WA, IL), they are often the sole decision-maker. |

In **solo and small practices (1-5 staff)**, the owner IS the decision-maker for marketing spend. There is no marketing department. In **mid-size practices (6-20 staff)**, a practice manager may handle vendor evaluation, but the owner/medical director approves budget.

### Services Offered (Typical Menu)

- Neurotoxins (Botox, Dysport, Xeomin)
- Dermal fillers (Juvederm, Restylane, RHA, Sculptra)
- Laser hair removal
- Laser skin resurfacing (Fraxel, CO2)
- CoolSculpting/body contouring
- Microneedling/RF microneedling (Morpheus8, SkinPen)
- Chemical peels
- IPL/photofacials
- Skin tightening (Ultherapy, Thermage)
- PRP/PRF facials
- IV therapy/vitamin drips
- Weight loss programs (semaglutide/tirzepatide), a rapidly growing category
- Hormone replacement therapy (BioTE pellets)

### The Cash-Pay Distinction

Med spas are **entirely cash-pay businesses**. No insurance billing, no claims, no reimbursement delays. Patients pay out of pocket for every procedure. This means:

- Revenue is immediate and predictable per appointment
- There is no insurance dependency or coding complexity
- The practice's growth is directly tied to marketing effectiveness
- Patients are comparing med spas to luxury retail brands, not insurance-based healthcare providers
- Price sensitivity is real but different: patients will pay premium prices for trust and results, but ad-driven leads often attract the wrong audience

### The Groupon/Deal-Seeker Problem

This is the single most painful topic in med spa marketing. Practices that have used Groupon, ClassPass, or heavy promotional discounting report:

- Deal-seeking patients almost never convert to full-price repeat clients
- A $500 treatment discounted 50% on Groupon yields only **$125** after Groupon's 50% cut
- Practices get trapped in the "Groupon cycle," running deals just to keep cash flowing, unable to break free
- Deal patients have higher complication/complaint rates and lower loyalty
- Price-shoppers who come through discount channels "ghost after the first inquiry" for full-price services
- Practices relying on deal-seekers average $200-300/visit versus $1,000-2,000+ for premium-positioned practices

**This is a core outreach angle.** Med spas that have been burned by Groupon understand the value of attracting high-intent, full-price patients through Google Ads. The pitch: stop competing on price, start capturing patients who are actively searching for your services and ready to pay.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

### Who They've Been Burned By

**Generic digital marketing agencies** that:
- Send monthly reports full of impressions and clicks but can't connect spend to actual bookings
- Don't understand med spa compliance, patient journey, or procedure-specific keyword strategy
- Run broad campaigns without negative keywords, burning through budget on irrelevant searches
- Lock practices into long-term contracts with no performance accountability
- Own the ad accounts, domains, or analytics, creating vendor lock-in

**Groupon/ClassPass/discount platforms** that:
- Attract bargain hunters who never become full-price patients
- Take 50% of already-discounted revenue
- Create operational chaos with unpredictable surges
- Degrade the practice's premium positioning

**RealSelf and directory listings** that:
- Charge for visibility with unclear ROI
- Generate comparison-shopping leads, not high-intent booking leads
- Mix your practice alongside every competitor in the area

**Lead generation companies** that:
- Sell shared leads to multiple practices simultaneously
- Generate leads from price-comparison keywords, resulting in tire-kickers
- Provide no transparency into lead source or quality

### Franchise Competition

Independent med spas compete against well-funded chains that dominate local search:

| Chain | Scale | Threat Level |
|---|---|---|
| Ideal Image | 150+ locations, national franchise expansion, significant brand awareness | High. Heavy Google Ads presence in every major metro. Recently closed some locations. |
| LaserAway | 100+ locations, 25+ dermatologists, national reach | High. Aggressive digital marketing and brick-and-mortar expansion. |
| SkinSpirit | 50+ locations, 20-year track record | Medium-high. Strong in West Coast and expanding. |
| Hand & Stone | 500+ franchise locations (spa + massage, expanding into medical aesthetics) | Medium. More traditional spa but adding injectable services. |
| Sono Bello | National body contouring chain | Medium. Niche competitor in CoolSculpting/body contouring space. |
| Skin Laundry | Growing chain focused on express laser and light treatments | Lower. Niche but growing. |

These chains have dedicated marketing teams, large ad budgets, and brand recognition. An independent med spa competing against them on Google Ads without expert management is fighting with one hand tied behind their back. This is a natural argument for professional PPC management.

### Private Equity Activity

17 notable med spa acquisitions documented as of April 2024, with growing investor interest. Examples include Trive Capital's investment in Formula Wellness. PE-backed rollups are accelerating consolidation, putting even more competitive pressure on independents who aren't marketing effectively.

## 7. PPCSauce Qualifying Criteria

| Criteria | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Minimum client-side ad spend | $10,000/month on Google Ads |
| Starting retainer | $2,500/month |
| Practice size sweet spot | Solo to mid-size (1-20 staff) |
| Revenue sweet spot | $500K-3M annual |
| Decision makers | Owner, Medical Director, Practice Manager |

**Disqualify if:**
- Ad spend below $10,000/month (insufficient data for meaningful optimization)
- Practice focused solely on low-ticket services (basic facials, waxing) without injectable/laser revenue
- Unwilling to implement conversion tracking (call tracking, GA4, form tracking)
- Recently opened (< 6 months), unless well-funded and scaling aggressively
- Franchise location without local marketing autonomy

**Additional pain points from ICP (not covered in VOC):**
- **Seasonal demand swings:** Summer (body contouring, laser hair removal) and holiday season (gift cards, injectable touch-ups) create surges that practices with poor campaign preparation miss entirely. Campaigns should adjust bids and budget seasonally.
- **Service-level campaign structure:** Most med spas run one generic campaign instead of dedicated campaigns for Botox, fillers, laser, body contouring, etc. Each service has different keyword intent, CPC, and patient value. Treating them identically wastes budget.
- **No-show problem:** 17-22% of appointments are no-shows (higher for new clients), costing $1,200-2,000/month. Practices that generate high-volume, low-quality leads amplify this problem. Better ad targeting = higher intent leads = lower no-show rates.
- **Phone-first booking:** Most med spa bookings happen via phone call, not form submission. Practices without call tracking are blind to which campaigns actually drive revenue. CallRail or equivalent is table stakes.
- **The upsell path:** A Botox patient who becomes a filler patient who becomes a laser patient who joins a membership represents $20,000+ in five-year value. PPC should target the entry point (typically Botox or a signature facial) knowing the practice's retention systems will extract the full LTV.
- **Compliance risk:** Google frequently disapproves healthcare ads for policy violations. Before-and-after imagery restrictions, medical claims limitations, and "unrealistic expectations" flags are constant headaches for med spa advertisers without expertise.

**Outreach positioning (from ICP):**
Med spa owners are acutely aware of the difference between a $200 Groupon facial and a $3,000 filler package. Position PPCSauce as the agency that uses campaign structure, keyword intent targeting, and landing page optimization to attract patients searching for premium treatments, not price shoppers comparing deals. The pitch lands hardest when framed as: "Stop paying for tire-kickers. Start capturing the patients who are already searching for what you offer and ready to book at full price."
