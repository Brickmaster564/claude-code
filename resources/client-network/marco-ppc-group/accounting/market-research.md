# Accounting: Market Research

**Date:** March 21, 2026
**Context:** B2B outreach targeting accounting firms as potential PPC clients for Marco (PPCScout). Not a PPL vertical. Research purpose: understand the ICP, find them, and write outreach that lands.

---

## 1. Market Overview

- **85,223** accounting services businesses operating in the US (IBISWorld, 2026), up 0.2% from 2025
- Market is extremely fragmented: the Big Four (Deloitte, EY, PwC, KPMG) dominate revenue, but **86% of all firms have fewer than 10 employees**
- Approximately **33,850 firms** are solo owner-operated practices with zero employees
- Solo and micro-firms (1-5 employees) make up the vast majority by count, while the top 5-10% of firms capture a disproportionate share of total industry profit
- Annual industry revenue: **$157.4 billion** (IBISWorld, 2026 estimate including all accounting services)
- Key services breakdown: tax preparation (individual and business), bookkeeping, payroll, audit, advisory/consulting, CFO services, forensic accounting
- The 2025 tax code reforms (OBBBA) drove increased demand for professional accounting guidance, expanding the addressable market for smaller firms picking up clients confused by the changes

## 3. ICP - Subgroup 1A: Tax and Accounting (Individual + Small Business)

**Who they are:**
- Solo practitioners and small firms (2-10 employees) offering tax preparation, bookkeeping, payroll, and general advisory services to individuals and small businesses
- Revenue ranges from ~$62,000/year (solo) to ~$292,000/year (2-5 employees) to ~$741,000/year (6-10 employees)
- Owner-operated in almost all cases: the CPA or EA is simultaneously the practitioner, the business owner, and the marketing decision-maker
- Concentrated in suburban and mid-market metros, though every US city has significant competition

**What they sell:**
- Individual tax preparation (1040s, state returns)
- Small business tax preparation and quarterly filings
- Monthly/quarterly bookkeeping
- Payroll processing
- Financial statements and reporting
- Tax planning and advisory
- IRS representation and audit defense
- Business entity formation and consulting

**Decision-maker profile:**
- Solo firms: the CPA or Enrolled Agent themselves, handling everything from client work to marketing with no dedicated staff for either
- Small firms (2-10): the founding/managing partner, who may delegate admin tasks to an office manager but retains full control of vendor selection and marketing budget
- Marketing sophistication is generally low: most firms rely on referrals (79% cite unsolicited client and professional referrals as their top source of new business), and fewer than 10% report advertising as a meaningful client acquisition channel
- They have been slow adopters of digital marketing, partly because referrals have historically worked and partly because they distrust agencies after seeing peers get burned
- Seasonal revenue concentration (January through April for tax season) creates budget anxiety around consistent monthly ad spend, even when the math supports it

**How they find vendors:**
- Peer referrals from other CPAs at professional associations (AICPA, state CPA societies)
- Google search when actively looking for help ("marketing for accountants," "PPC for CPA firms")
- Industry events and conferences (AICPA Engage, state society annual meetings)
- Cold outreach actually works in this market because so few agencies target it with real specificity, unlike the oversaturated legal market

## 4. ICP - Subgroup 1B: Specialized/Niche Accounting

**Who they are:**
- Firms that focus on a specific industry vertical or service line: construction accounting, medical/dental practice accounting, real estate accounting, nonprofit accounting, ecommerce bookkeeping, CFO advisory, or forensic accounting
- Typically 3-15 employees, more established than generalist solo shops
- Higher revenue per client because they solve more complex, industry-specific problems

**What they sell:**
- Industry-specific tax strategy (cost segregation for real estate, R&D tax credits for tech)
- Fractional/outsourced CFO services ($2,000-$7,000/month retainers)
- Financial modeling and forecasting
- M&A due diligence and advisory
- Forensic accounting and litigation support

**Why they're distinct as PPC prospects:**
- They are already competing in niches with defined keyword clusters ("construction CPA," "dental practice bookkeeper," "startup CFO"), which makes PPC campaigns cleaner and higher-intent
- Their client lifetime values are significantly higher: a construction company paying $3,000-$5,000/month for ongoing accounting and CFO services represents $36,000-$60,000/year in recurring revenue
- They tend to be more marketing-aware than generalist tax shops because they actively position themselves as specialists and often already have websites, content, and some digital presence
- Decision-makers are still typically the founder/managing partner, but they are more likely to have a marketing budget line item and some experience evaluating agency proposals
- The pitch for PPC is even stronger here because they can quantify exactly what a new client is worth and justify the spend without guessing

## 5. Competitive Landscape (Agencies/Vendors They've Dealt With)

**Major vendors and their reputations:**

| Vendor | Primary Complaint |
|---|---|
| Build Your Firm | Exclusively targets accounting firms with website + marketing packages. Mixed reviews: some find value, others feel locked into templates with minimal customization. |
| CPA Site Solutions | Built by CPAs, sells websites and SEO bundles. Complaints center on outdated designs and slow results. |
| Inovautus Consulting | Consulting-heavy, expensive, aimed at larger firms. Small firms feel priced out or ignored after the initial engagement. |
| WebFX / generic agencies | Not accounting-specific. Clients report cookie-cutter campaigns where the agency doesn't understand seasonal tax dynamics, compliance language, or the difference between individual and business accounting keywords. |
| Scorpion | Aggressive in legal but expanding into adjacent professional services. Same complaints follow them: long contracts, proprietary lock-in, declining service quality after the sale. |

**The trust dynamic:**

Accounting professionals are inherently analytical and skeptical, which makes them harder to pitch but more loyal once trust is established. The pattern of bad experiences breaks down into three categories.

First, agencies that don't understand seasonality: accounting is one of the most seasonal businesses in existence, and any PPC vendor who runs flat-rate campaigns year-round with no adjustment for tax season peaks and summer valleys immediately loses credibility.

Second, agencies that hold accounts hostage: CPAs are accustomed to owning client data and work product, so any vendor that builds campaigns in a proprietary account the client can't access triggers immediate distrust. Marco's full account ownership and transparency positioning directly counters this.

Third, agencies with vague reporting: accountants live in numbers and reconciliation. They expect clear attribution between ad spend, leads generated, and clients signed. Agencies that report on impressions and clicks without tying them to actual new business get fired quickly.

Marco's offer is structurally designed to overcome all three objections: performance guarantee removes downside risk, no contracts eliminate lock-in fear, full account ownership addresses the transparency gap, and CRO inclusion (landing pages + intake flow) demonstrates that the agency understands the full funnel rather than just running ads into a broken website.

## 6. Apollo Search Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Industry | Accounting, Financial Services |
| Organization keywords | accounting firm, CPA firm, tax preparation, bookkeeping, enrolled agent, tax advisory, certified public accountant, accounting services |
| Person titles | CPA, Owner, Founder, Managing Partner, Partner, Principal, Sole Proprietor, Tax Director, Practice Owner |
| Company size | 1-50 employees |
| Location | United States |
| Seniority | owner, partner, c_suite, founder |
| Exclude | Big Four, Top 100 firm, staffing agency, recruiting, software company, law firm |

**Key exclusions (post-search filter):**
- Big Four and Top 100 firms (they have in-house marketing departments and enterprise agency relationships)
- Accounting staffing/recruiting companies (not actual practices)
- Accounting software companies (Intuit, Xero, FreshBooks)
- Government and nonprofit accounting offices (no marketing budget for client acquisition)
- Firms with no web presence at all (too early-stage for PPC, better served by a website-first engagement)

## 7. Top Target Metros

Ranked by accountant density, competition level, and Google Ads activity for accounting services:

1. **New York City, NY** - Financial capital with the highest CPA density in the country. CPCs are premium, but client values match. Thousands of small firms competing for high-net-worth and small business clients.
2. **Los Angeles, CA** - Accounting is the largest professional services employer in LA (14%+ of the sector). High cost of living drives higher fee structures and bigger PPC budgets.
3. **Houston, TX** - Second only to NYC for independent accounting opportunities, fueled by the energy sector and a massive small business ecosystem. Lower cost of living means PPC budget goes further.
4. **Chicago, IL** - Over 50,000 accountants and auditors, with a strong mid-market firm presence. Competitive but accessible CPCs relative to coastal markets.
5. **Dallas-Fort Worth, TX** - Rapid population growth and business formation create steady demand for accounting services. Emerging firms are actively investing in digital acquisition.
6. **Atlanta, GA** - Southeast hub for professional services, with strong small business growth and increasing PPC competition among mid-size CPA firms.
7. **Phoenix, AZ** - Fast-growing metro with a surge of relocated businesses from CA. Many newer firms are digitally native and already running some form of ads.
8. **Miami, FL** - High concentration of international business clients driving demand for specialized tax and advisory services. Bilingual keyword opportunities add another layer.
9. **Denver, CO** - Strong startup and small business economy with tech-savvy firm owners who are more likely to understand and invest in PPC.
10. **Nashville, TN** - One of the fastest-growing metros in the US for small business formation. CPA competition is intensifying but most firms are still relying on referrals, which creates a first-mover advantage for PPC.

Mid-sized markets like Austin, Charlotte, Tampa, San Diego, and Minneapolis are also worth targeting because CPCs are lower, firm owners are less jaded by agency pitches, and the PPC opportunity is often untapped entirely.
