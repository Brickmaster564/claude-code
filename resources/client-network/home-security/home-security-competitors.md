## TIER 1: Pure Lead-Gen Quote Aggregators (Your Direct Competitors)

These are the sites operating the exact model you're in — they collect homeowner info via a free quote form and sell/route those leads to security companies. These are the ones to study hardest.

---

### 1. US Home Security Quotes (ushomesecurityquotes.com)

**What They Do:** Multi-quote comparison platform. Homeowner fills out a form, gets matched to multiple alarm companies who compete on price.

**Headline Copy & Framing:**
- Primary headline: "Free Home Security Quotes"
- Subhead: "Compare Free Home Security Quotes From Top Alarm System Providers"
- Savings hook: "Save Up To 50% Off On Your Security System"
- Method framing: "Simply comparing free quotes by local alarm system providers"

**How They Entice the Lead:**
- The savings percentage (50%) is the anchor — it gives the visitor a concrete financial reason to fill out the form rather than going direct to a single provider.
- They name-drop recognizable brands (Vivint, Protection One) to build credibility that these aren't fly-by-night installers.
- The "compare" framing positions the homeowner as the smart shopper, not the person being sold to. This is a critical psychological lever — the visitor feels in control.
- Coverage scope messaging: "24/7 Burglary, Fire, Flood, and Medical Protection" — expands the perceived value beyond just break-ins.

**Copy Patterns to Note:**
- Heavy use of "free" — it appears in the page title, the headline, and the CTA
- "Compare" is the action verb, not "buy" or "sign up"
- Savings framing is percentage-based (50%), not dollar-based — percentages feel larger on big-ticket items
- Brand logos serve as social proof without testimonials

**Strengths:** Simple value prop, savings-first framing, brand-name trust transfer
**Weaknesses:** Dated design (based on search snippets), likely low SEO authority compared to review sites, thin content

### 2. HomeSafety.com (homesafety.com)

**What They Do:** Positions itself as "the leading independent provider of home security quotes." Homeowner fills out a needs assessment form, gets matched with local security companies.

**Headline Copy & Framing:**
- Primary headline: "Get FREE Home Security Quotes"
- Positioning: "The Leading Independent Provider of Home Security Quotes Trusted by Homeowners Nationwide"
- Process framing: "Simply fill out our form describing your security needs, and we will provide competitive quotes from security companies in your area"

**How They Entice the Lead:**
- The "independent" positioning is the key differentiator — it implies they're not pushing one brand, which builds trust.
- "Trusted by homeowners nationwide" is authority language designed to reduce friction. No specific number or testimonial, but the claim is broad enough to feel credible.
- The process is framed as low-effort: "Simply fill out our form" — the word "simply" does heavy lifting to minimize perceived commitment.
- "In your area" creates local relevance — the homeowner isn't getting generic national pricing, they're getting quotes from companies that actually serve their zip code.

**Copy Patterns to Note:**
- "FREE" is capitalized — visual emphasis in a text-heavy page
- "Independent" and "trusted" are the two trust-building adjectives doing the most work
- "Competitive quotes" implies the homeowner will get below-market pricing through competition
- No mention of how many quotes they'll receive (unlike others who promise "3 quotes")

**Strengths:** Strong trust positioning, "independent" framing is genuinely differentiating, clean value prop
**Weaknesses:** No specificity on number of quotes, no named brands, no savings percentage anchor

## COPY & FRAMING ANALYSIS: PATTERNS ACROSS ALL SITES

### The 5 Copy Levers Every Site Uses (In Order of Frequency)

1. **"Free" / "No Cost" / "No Obligation"** — Every single site leads with this. It's table stakes. The variation is in how they say it: "FREE" (caps), "complimentary" (premium), "100% free" (emphatic), "completely free" (absolute).

2. **"Compare" / "Competing Quotes"** — The comparison frame is universal. It positions the homeowner as a smart shopper and implies savings without requiring a specific discount number.

3. **"Save [X]%"** — When used, it's always a large number (50%, 75%). These are aspirational anchors, not guarantees. Only the more aggressive sites use specific percentages.

4. **"Local" / "In Your Area"** — Geographic relevance reduces the abstract feeling of an online form. The homeowner knows they'll hear from companies that actually serve them.

5. **"Top" / "Trusted" / "Best"** — Superlatives are everywhere. They're the weakest lever but still do work as ambient credibility signals.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR YOUR SITE

### What the Best Sites Do That You Should Steal

1. **Lead with a specific savings number.** "Save up to 50%" outperforms "save money." Anchor high.

2. **Promise a specific number of quotes.** "Get 3 quotes" > "get quotes." Specificity builds trust and sets expectations.

3. **Include a speed/effort guarantee.** "2 minutes" or "takes 60 seconds" — time-to-value messaging dramatically reduces form abandonment.

4. **Frame the form as "comparing" not "requesting."** The homeowner should feel like they're shopping, not filling out a lead form. "Compare" is the magic word.

5. **Name-drop brands.** If your buyers include ADT, Vivint, or SimpliSafe authorized dealers, put those logos on your page. It transfers trust instantly.

6. **Add "professionally monitored" language explicitly.** Most of these sites are generic about what kind of security. If your buyer model is specifically professional monitoring (subscription/RMR), make that the focus. You can differentiate by being the site that specifically matches homeowners with monitored alarm systems, not DIY kits.

7. **Address the pain point directly.** "Stop calling 5 different companies" or "No more sitting through sales presentations" — name the bad experience and position your form as the escape.

### Gaps You Can Exploit

Most of these sites are thin on content, dated in design, and generic in their targeting. None of them specifically position around the professional monitoring buyer — they all lump monitored and DIY together. A site that explicitly says "We only match you with professionally monitored alarm systems with 24/7 central station monitoring" would stand out and attract a more qualified lead for your buyer at the $60–$100 CPL range.

**Scale:** 6+ million monitored homes and businesses in the US. By far the largest.

**How They Buy Leads:**

ADT buys the customer contract and pays the dealer weekly compensation plus continuing equity/revenue share. They run their own lead gen AND have a partner/affiliate program paying up to $350–$400+ per connection/sale. If you sell leads to SafeStreets dealers, you're feeding the ADT machine.

**What They Pay For:**
- Professionally monitored alarm leads (their core product)

- Both residential and commercial

**Why They Matter to You:** ADT's dealer network is the single largest pool of lead buyers in the US home security space. If you're generating leads at $60–$100 CPL, ADT dealers are your most natural buyer because they recoup that cost through the RMR (recurring monthly revenue) from 24–36 month monitoring contracts. The economics work because ADT buys the account from the dealer at a multiple of the monthly monitoring fee.

**Affiliate/Partner Payout:** SafeStreets pays up to $400+ per sale through their partner program. This means your $60–$100 lead, if it converts, generates $400+ for the referring partner — healthy margin.
