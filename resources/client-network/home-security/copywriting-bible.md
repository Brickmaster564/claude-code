## 11. Under-the-Fingernail Copy Blocks

**Block 1: The Noise at Night**

You're lying in bed. It's 2am. And you hear it — a sound downstairs that doesn't belong. Your heart slams into your chest. You hold your breath. Your partner shifts beside you. Your kids are asleep down the hall. And in that frozen moment, you realize: you have nothing. No alarm. No cameras. No way to know if someone is actually inside your house or if it's just the wind. So you lie there, staring at the ceiling, listening, waiting, hoping it's nothing — because if it's something, you have no plan.

**Block 2: The Aftermath**

They told her it happened between noon and 3pm. Broad daylight. She was at work. The kids were at school. They pried open the back door — took about 15 seconds. They took the laptops, the jewelry, some cash from the bedroom drawer. But that's not what broke her. It was coming home to the open drawers. The footprints on the carpet. The feeling that someone had been in her bedroom, touched her things, gone through her kids' rooms. She couldn't sleep in that house for weeks. She'd lie awake, flinching at every sound, checking the locks over and over. Her daughter started sleeping with the lights on. Her son didn't want to be home alone anymore. They got a security system the next day. She says the same thing everyone says: "I should have done this years ago."

**Block 3: The Traveling Parent**

He travels three weeks a month for work. Hotels, airports, rental cars. And every single night in some anonymous hotel room, the same thought creeps in: Are they safe? He pictures his wife at home, alone with the kids, and he thinks about all the things that could go wrong. A stranger at the door. A noise in the garage. A window left unlocked. He texts her every night: "Everything okay?" And she always says yes. But he can feel the weight in those three letters. She doesn't feel safe. She just doesn't want him to worry. One $15/month decision would let him check every camera, every sensor, every door — from his phone, from anywhere. He'd know, not hope, that they're protected.
