# 1. The Home Security Industry

The home security industry encompasses products, services, and monitoring solutions designed to protect residential properties from burglary, intrusion, fire, and environmental hazards.

Core functions include:
- Alarm system installation and maintenance
- 24/7 professional monitoring and emergency dispatch
- Video surveillance and smart doorbell cameras
- Smart home integration (locks, lights, sensors)
- DIY self-monitored security kits

The industry spans two primary models:
- **Professional monitoring** — Equipment installed (professionally or DIY) with a monthly subscription for 24/7 monitoring center dispatch. Costs range $15–$53/month.
- **Self-monitored / DIY** — Consumer-owned equipment with app-based alerts; no monitoring center. Lower cost, but the homeowner is responsible for calling 911.

# 3. Types of Home Security Services

## Professional Monitoring (Traditional)

24/7 monitoring center watches alarm signals, dispatches police/fire when triggered. Typically requires contract (12–60 months). Examples: ADT, Vivint, Brinks Home.

## DIY Self-Monitored

Consumer installs equipment, receives alerts on phone, decides whether to call authorities. No contract required. Examples: SimpliSafe, Ring Alarm, Abode.

## Video Surveillance / Smart Cameras

Standalone or integrated camera systems with cloud storage. Often subscription-gated for playback and AI features. Examples: Ring, Arlo, Nest/Google, Reolink.

## Smart Home Security

Integrated ecosystems combining cameras, door/window sensors, smart locks, motion detectors, smoke/CO detectors, and home automation — controlled via a single app. Examples: ADT + Google Nest, Vivint Smart Home, Apple HomeKit-compatible systems.

## Commercial / Small Business Security

Overlapping market — many residential providers also serve small businesses. Higher ticket, longer contracts, more complex installations.

# 4. Key Terminology

## Monitored System
Alarm system connected to a central monitoring station that dispatches emergency services.

## Self-Monitored System
System that sends alerts directly to the homeowner's phone; no professional dispatch.

## False Alarm
An alarm triggered without an actual emergency. 95% of residential alarm activations are false alarms. Many municipalities fine repeat offenders.

## Central Station
The monitoring facility that receives alarm signals 24/7 and contacts authorities.

## Contact Sensors
Magnetic sensors placed on doors/windows that trigger when opened.

## Motion Detector
PIR (passive infrared) sensor detecting movement in a room or zone.

## Smart Lock
Keyless electronic lock controlled via app, code, or voice assistant.

## Cellular Backup
Alarm communication via cellular network when Wi-Fi or landline fails.

## Monitoring Contract
Agreement committing customer to monthly monitoring fees for a set term (typically 12–60 months). Early termination fees can reach $2,500+ (e.g., Vivint's 100% remaining balance penalty).

# 5. The Lead Buyers

Primary buyers seek homeowners interested in purchasing or upgrading a security system — ideally homeowners (not renters), with household income above $50K, and families with children.

## Categories

### National Security Companies & Their Dealer Networks

The largest buyers of home security leads. They operate authorized dealer programs where independent contractors sell and install under the brand.

Examples:
- **ADT** — Largest player (~14% market share). Extensive authorized dealer network.
- **Vivint** — Premium smart home security. Direct sales force + dealer channel.
- **Brinks Home Security** — Dealer-driven model.
- **Safe Haven** — Largest ADT authorized dealer.

### Regional / Independent Alarm Companies

Local and regional installers building their customer base. Often buy leads from aggregators or run local SEO/PPC.

### DIY Security Brands

SimpliSafe, Ring, Cove — primarily acquire customers through direct-to-consumer digital marketing rather than buying third-party leads, but some use affiliate/lead-gen partnerships.

### Lead Aggregators & Brokers

Companies that generate and sell leads to the above buyers.

Examples:
- Payperlead.com (3,000+ calls and 4,000+ web leads per day)
- Exclusive Leads Agency
- 33 Mile Radius
- DOPPCALL (pay-per-call model)
- Marketcall
- Billy.com

Buyers track:
- CPL (Cost Per Lead) — Home services average ~$92; home security likely $75–$150 depending on lead type and exclusivity
- CPA (Cost Per Acquisition)
- ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)
- Appointment rate (~25% average from lead providers)
- One provider claims $2,000 revenue per $100 ad spend over time

# 7. Regulatory Landscape

The home security lead gen and sales industry is regulated at federal, state, and local levels.

## Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

- **Telemarketing Sales Rule (TSR):** Any telemarketing outreach must comply. Violations carry penalties up to $50,120 per occurrence.
- **Door-to-door scam warnings:** FTC has specifically warned consumers about deceptive home security door-to-door sales tactics — high-pressure pitches, fake "free equipment" offers that lock consumers into expensive long-term contracts.
- **Cooling-Off Rule:** Consumers have 3 days to cancel purchases made from door-to-door salespeople.

## State & Local Regulations

- Many states require door-to-door salespeople to display photo ID and state their company name upfront.
- Alarm permit requirements vary by municipality. Some cities require a permit before a system can be activated.
- False alarm fines are common — many jurisdictions fine homeowners after repeated false alarm dispatches.

## Advertising Restrictions

- No guarantees of preventing break-ins
- No misleading claims about police response times
- No false urgency ("crime wave in your area") without substantiation
- Must disclose contract terms, monitoring fees, and cancellation penalties clearly
